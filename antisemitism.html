IntroductionHigh tension

How Christian theology enabled the persecution of Jewish people

Christianity emerged from Judaism. Jesus was Jewish, as were all his first disciples and the authors of most of the New Testament. Yet within a few centuries, Christian theology developed doctrines and practices that would make Christian-majority societies among the most dangerous places in history to be Jewish. This is not a marginal or peripheral issue — it sits at the heart of the church's self-understanding and its relationship to its own Scriptures.

The Texts That Were WeaponizedGreek study

Matthew 27:25 and John 8:44 — how they were read

"All the people answered, 'His blood is on us and on our children!'"Matthew 27:25
πᾶς ὁ λαόςpas ho laosGreek

"All the people" — a Jerusalem crowd, not the Jewish people collectively across history

This verse became the primary scriptural foundation for the "blood guilt" accusation — the claim that Jewish people collectively and across generations bear guilt for the death of Jesus. For over a millennium, it was used to justify violence, forced conversion, expulsion, and massacre. The theological problems are multiple: (1) laos refers to a specific crowd in Jerusalem, not the Jewish people as a whole across history; (2) collective intergenerational guilt for an ancestral act is not a concept found elsewhere in the New Testament; (3) Romans 11 explicitly affirms that God has not rejected the Jewish people. The Second Vatican Council (Nostra Aetate, 1965) formally repudiated the collective blood-guilt interpretation.

"You belong to your father, the devil, and you want to carry out your father's desires."John 8:44

The context critical for reading John 8:44

This harsh statement is part of a heated dispute between Jesus and specific Jewish leaders challenging his authority — a genre of intra-Jewish polemic well-documented in Jewish texts of the period (the Dead Sea Scrolls contain equally sharp language between Jewish groups). Scholars are nearly unanimous that these statements were not intended as — and cannot rightly be read as — characterizations of Jewish people as a whole or across time. The fact that Jesus and his interlocutors are all Jewish is itself the clearest evidence against a racial or ethnic reading.

What HappenedHistorical record

Christian antisemitism through history

📚
4th century AD

John Chrysostom's Adversus Judaeos

Eight sermons by one of the most celebrated early church fathers contain rhetoric against Jewish people that later scholars have described as the most virulent anti-Jewish literature of late antiquity. Chrysostom called synagogues "dens of thieves" and described Jewish people in terms that would echo through subsequent Christian preaching. His enormous influence meant this language was reproduced and amplified for centuries.

✝️
1096, 1146, 1190

Crusade massacres of Jewish communities

Crusading armies, en route to "liberate" Jerusalem, massacred Jewish communities throughout the Rhineland, England, and France. The logic was direct: if we are fighting Christ's enemies in Jerusalem, why spare his enemies at home? These were not rogue atrocities but were theologically motivated by teachings the soldiers had received in church.

📜
1543

Martin Luther, On the Jews and Their Lies

Late in his life, Luther published virulently antisemitic material calling for the burning of synagogues and the expulsion of Jews from Germany — a text that, appallingly, would be reprinted by Nazi publishers in the 1930s as theological cover. This is among the most painful facts about the Protestant Reformation's legacy and has required explicit repudiation by Lutheran churches worldwide.

🔶
1939–1945

The Holocaust and Christian complicity

The Holocaust was perpetrated in a continent that had been Christian for over a millennium. Most of the perpetrators were baptized. The churches' response was, with noble individual exceptions (rescuers were disproportionately motivated by religious conviction), largely one of silence, accommodation, or complicity. The theological antisemitism embedded in Christian culture did not cause the Holocaust — Nazi ideology was not simply Christianity — but it created a cultural soil in which it was possible.

Scholarly Perspectives

What historians and theologians say

Christian antisemitism is not a distortion of the New Testament or an aberration from the Christian tradition — it is a strand woven from the tradition's own texts, genuinely drawn from them, however badly read. The only adequate response is not to pretend it was always misreading but to go back to those texts, read them better, and insist on the better reading with theological seriousness.

Rosemary Radford RuetherGarrett-Evangelical Theological Seminary; author of Faith and Fratricide: The Theological Roots of Anti-Semitism (1974)

Romans 9–11, the most sustained New Testament treatment of the Jewish people's theological status, unambiguously affirms that God has not rejected Israel: "all Israel will be saved" (Romans 11:26). Any Christian theology that ends in contempt for or persecution of Jewish people has failed to read Paul — and has failed to read the Gospel that Paul's own life was shaped by.

N.T. WrightUniversity of St Andrews; author of Paul and the Faithfulness of God (2013)

Nostra Aetate (Vatican II, 1965) is among the most consequential documents in modern Catholic history. Its declaration that the Jewish people cannot be held collectively responsible for Jesus's death, and its explicit condemnation of antisemitism, represented a formal theological rupture with centuries of precedent that required real courage.

Edward KesslerWoolf Institute, Cambridge; author of What Do Jews and Christians Share?

The Theological Reckoning

What the church must say — and do

Supercessionism / Replacement Theology

The church has replaced Israel in God's purposes

The traditional view holding that the church, as the "new Israel," has inherited all the covenant promises originally made to ethnic Israel — whose distinctive place in God's purposes has ended. In its hard form, this has historically contributed directly to antisemitism. Many theologians now distinguish "hard" supercessionism (condemned) from "soft" (debated). Dominant in much of Western Christian history; increasingly questioned since the Holocaust.

Dual Covenant Theology

Judaism retains a valid covenant relationship with God apart from Jesus

Jewish people already stand in a valid covenant with God through Torah and are not in need of Christian evangelism. Promoted as a theologically grounded way of honoring Jewish dignity. Criticized by many Christians and Jewish thinkers alike as theologically inconsistent with core Christian claims about Jesus.

Covenantal Continuity

God's covenant with Israel is irrevocable; the church is grafted in

Romans 11:29 — "the gifts and calling of God are irrevocable" — means the church has not replaced Israel but has been incorporated into Israel's covenant through Jesus. Israel retains its election; the church is a wild branch grafted onto the olive tree of Israel. The appropriate stance toward Jewish people is gratitude, not condescension. N.T. Wright; many contemporary evangelical and Reformed theologians.

Repentance and Solidarity

Christian solidarity with Jewish people requires acknowledging the church's specific historical culpability

Formal repudiations — Vatican II's Nostra Aetate, the Lutheran World Federation's 1983 repudiation of Luther's antisemitic writings, multiple evangelical statements — represent necessary but not sufficient beginnings of a reckoning that must also shape Christian preaching, education, and political witness against contemporary antisemitism.
