What Happens to Infants Who Die?

← Heaven, Hell & Salvation

Section 1

IntroductionPastorally urgent

One of the most pastorally pressing questions in theology

Few theological questions carry more immediate pastoral weight than this one. Parents who have lost an infant or experienced miscarriage often need a real, careful answer — not platitudes. The Bible never directly and systematically addresses the question, which has led to a genuine range of thoughtful Christian responses.

The Central Biblical Clue

David's words about his dying child

"But now that he is dead, why should I go on fasting? Can I bring him back again? I will go to him, but he will not return to me."2 Samuel 12:23

After his infant son with Bathsheba dies, David — who had been fasting and weeping while the child was ill — immediately ceases mourning and declares "I will go to him." This is widely read across the theological spectrum as David's confident expectation of reunion with his child in the afterlife, making it the single most direct biblical text bearing on the question, even though it is brief and not presented as systematic doctrine.

A caution on over-reading this verse

Some scholars note that "I will go to him" could, in its most minimal sense, simply mean "I too will die eventually" (i.e., join him in death/Sheol generally) rather than necessarily implying a confident expectation of joyful reunion specifically in a blessed afterlife. Most commentators across traditions, however, read David's changed emotional state — from desperate fasting to calm acceptance — as implying more than mere shared mortality; it suggests genuine hope.

The Underlying Theological Problem

Why this question is theologically complicated

The difficulty arises from the intersection of two doctrines: the universality of sin (Romans 3:23, "all have sinned") and salvation by conscious faith (Romans 10:9, "if you declare with your mouth... and believe in your heart"). An infant has neither personally sinned by conscious choice nor exercised conscious faith — leaving their status genuinely underdetermined by the most commonly cited soteriological texts.

דַּעַת טוֹב וָרָעda'at tov varaHebrew

"Knowledge of good and evil" — used in Isaiah and Deuteronomy regarding moral capacity

Isaiah 7:15–16 and Deuteronomy 1:39 both reference children "who do not yet know good from evil" as a recognized category — biblical language that has been foundational for the popular "age of accountability" doctrine, the idea that children below a certain threshold of moral understanding are not held accountable for sin in the same way morally capable adults are. This is an inference from these texts, however, not a fully systematic doctrine that Scripture states explicitly and directly addresses to the question of infant death and salvation.

Scholarly Perspectives

What theologians say

I believe Scripture gives good and sufficient grounds for confidence that those who die in infancy are received into God's grace and saved by Christ's atoning work, applied to them apart from the conscious faith they were never capable of exercising. This is not presumption but a reasonable inference from God's revealed character of justice and mercy.

R.A. WebbPresbyterian theologian; author of The Theology of Infant Salvation (1907), a classic Reformed treatment

The doctrine of limbo — a state of natural, but not supernatural, happiness for unbaptized infants — was never formally and infallibly defined Catholic dogma, only a permitted theological hypothesis. The 2007 International Theological Commission document The Hope of Salvation for Infants Who Die Without Being Baptized explicitly expressed grounds for hope that such infants are saved, reflecting real doctrinal development on this question even within Catholic theology.

International Theological CommissionVatican document, 2007, approved by Pope Benedict XVI for publication

Strict adherence to the Westminster Confession's careful wording — that "elect infants dying in infancy" are saved (not "all infants") — has led some in the Reformed tradition to a more cautious, less universally confident position than is popularly assumed; the Confession does not claim all infants are elect, only that elect ones who die in infancy are saved by Christ through the Spirit.

Westminster Confession of FaithChapter 10.3 (1646), with later interpretive commentary by Reformed theologians

Christian Positions

Five Christian positions

Universal Infant Salvation

All who die in infancy are saved

Drawing on David's hope in 2 Samuel 12:23, Christ's special blessing of children ("let the little children come to me," Matthew 19:14), and the "age of accountability" inference, most evangelical and many Reformed theologians hold that all infants who die are received into God's grace through Christ. The broad consensus among Protestant pastors today, regardless of formal denominational statements.

Elect Infants Only

Only infants who are among God's elect are saved

Strict confessional Calvinism (following the precise wording of the Westminster Confession 10.3) affirms that "elect infants" dying in infancy are saved through Christ and the Spirit — without claiming this necessarily includes every infant universally, maintaining consistency with unconditional election doctrine.

Catholic: Hope, Not Certain Doctrine

The church expresses confident hope, while not formally defining the question

Historically, unbaptized infants were sometimes thought to go to "limbo" (natural but not supernatural happiness) due to the stain of original sin without baptismal cleansing. The 2007 Vatican commission moved toward "an attitude of trustful prayer" for their salvation, reflecting genuine doctrinal development without issuing infallible dogmatic certainty either way.

Mystery / Reverent Agnosticism

Scripture does not give us enough to be fully certain

Some theologians hold that while we can trust God's perfect justice and goodness, Scripture simply does not provide enough explicit material to construct full certainty on this question — and that reverent trust in God's character, rather than systematic doctrinal confidence, is the most honest response.

See: Webb, The Theology of Infant Salvation (1907); International Theological Commission, The Hope of Salvation for Infants Who Die Without Being Baptized (2007); Sproul, Saved from What?.
