IntroductionGrowing urgency

What does the Bible say about humanity's relationship to creation?

Genesis 1:28 gives humanity "dominion" over creation — a word that has been used to justify everything from responsible stewardship to unlimited exploitation. The question of what it means to rule creation faithfully has become one of the most practically consequential theological debates of our era.

Original LanguageHebrew study

Two different creation mandates — "rule" and "tend"

רָדָהradahHebrew

"Dominion / rule" — Genesis 1:28

Genesis 1:28 commands humans to "have dominion" (radah) over living creatures. The word is used elsewhere for royal rule over subjects (1 Kings 4:24, Psalms 72:8) — a position of authority, not ownership. Critically, in the ancient Near Eastern context, a king's "dominion" was understood as a stewardship responsibility, not a license for exploitation: the good king cares for, protects, and provides for those under his rule. The text assumes human authority corresponds to human accountability.

עָבַד וְשָׁמַרabad ve-shamarHebrew

"To serve / till" and "to keep / guard" — Genesis 2:15

The second creation account (Genesis 2) uses completely different vocabulary: humans are placed in the garden "to work it and take care of it" — abad (serve, till, even "worship" in other contexts) and shamar (guard, protect, keep). These are not words of domination but of service and protection. Many scholars note that abad (serve) literally reverses the power dynamic: humanity serves the garden rather than ruling it. These two texts together — "rule" and "serve" — define the paradox at the heart of the creation mandate.

"The earth is the LORD's, and everything in it, the world, and all who live in it."Psalm 24:1

Psalm 24:1's claim that the earth belongs to God, not to humanity, frames the entire creation mandate as stewardship rather than ownership. Humans have authority over creation but do not own it — they are tenants managing God's property.

Historical ControversyHistorical record

Lynn White's famous 1967 challenge to Christianity

📄
1967

Lynn White Jr., "The Historical Roots of Our Ecologic Crisis," Science

Historian Lynn White published a landmark article arguing that the Judeo-Christian doctrine of dominion was directly responsible for the Western environmental crisis — by desacralizing nature (stripping it of spirit or divine presence), Christianity had legitimized its exploitation. The article sparked enormous controversy and became one of the most widely cited and debated essays in environmental ethics. While White's specific historical causation argument has been contested (pre-Christian cultures also exploited nature; other cultures with different theologies have done the same), the challenge forced Christian theologians to reckon seriously with Genesis 1:28 and its legacy.

📚
1970

Francis Schaeffer, Pollution and the Death of Man

One of the first prominent evangelical responses to environmental concern, Schaeffer argued that a biblical view of creation — nature as genuinely real and created good by God, distinct from both pantheism (which deifies nature) and secular materialism (which reduces it to resource) — provides uniquely strong grounds for environmental stewardship. Evangelicals, he argued, should be the most consistent environmentalists, not the least.

🌿
2015

Pope Francis, Laudato Si'

This landmark encyclical argued that environmental care and care for the poor are theologically inseparable — "integral ecology." The cry of the earth and the cry of the poor arise from the same root: a throwaway culture that treats both creation and the vulnerable as expendable. The encyclical was significant for addressing climate change explicitly as a moral and theological issue, not only a scientific one.

Scholarly Perspectives

What theologians say about creation care

The ecological crisis is, at its theological root, a crisis of idolatry — the replacement of the Creator with creation's products as the source of meaning and security. Christian environmentalism flows from worship of the Creator, not from a romantic attachment to nature for its own sake.

N.T. WrightUniversity of St Andrews; author of Surprised by Hope (2008), addressing new creation and ecological responsibility

Lynn White's critique identified a real problem — a misreading of Genesis that has contributed to exploitation — but misidentified its source. The problem is not the Bible but a specific hermeneutical tradition that elevated dominion and ignored servanthood. Scripture properly read is one of environmentalism's strongest foundations.

Richard BauckhamUniversity of St Andrews; author of Bible and Ecology (2010)

The eschatological vision of Revelation 21–22 is not the destruction of creation but its renewal — a new heaven and earth, not an escape from earth. Christian hope is therefore not indifferent to what happens to this creation; it is invested in creation's future, not its disposal.

Jürgen MoltmannUniversity of Tübingen; author of God in Creation (1985)

Evangelical climate skepticism reflects political partisanship masquerading as theological principle. There is no serious exegetical basis for the belief that concern for climate change contradicts biblical faith — and considerable basis for the view that responsible stewardship requires taking the scientific consensus seriously.

Katharine HayhoeTexas Tech University; atmospheric scientist; evangelical Christian; author of Saving Us (2021)

Christian Positions

Major Christian responses to creation and ecology

Dominion Theology

Human authority over creation is largely unlimited for human benefit

Genesis 1:28's dominion command grants humans broad authority to use natural resources for human flourishing. Environmental regulation is often treated with suspicion as limiting God-given human freedom and market activity. Associated with some prosperity theology and Christian nationalist movements; a minority position among professional Christian ethicists.

Stewardship Model

Humans are managers, not owners; accountability to God is real

The "abad and shamar" mandate, Psalm 24:1's claim that the earth is God's, and the parable of the talents all point to humans as stewards rather than owners. We have genuine authority, but will answer to the true Owner for how we used it. The mainstream evangelical and broader Protestant position.

Integral Ecology

Care for creation and care for the poor are inseparable

Pope Francis's framework explicitly connects ecological destruction with the exploitation of the poor — both stem from the same commodification logic that treats creation and human dignity as expendable resources. Environmental ethics and social justice cannot be pursued independently. Catholic Social Teaching; increasingly influential in mainline Protestant ethics.

Eschatological Indifference

This creation will be destroyed anyway; prioritize souls

If the physical world will ultimately be destroyed and replaced by a new creation, investing heavily in environmental preservation reflects misplaced priorities. Evangelizing people matters more than saving ecosystems. This view is criticized by many evangelical scholars as resting on a misreading of biblical eschatology — Revelation 21–22 describes renewal, not annihilation.
