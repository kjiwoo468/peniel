A History of Faith-Science Conflicts

← Faith & Science

Section 1

Introduction

Two books, one Author

Medieval theologians spoke of two "books" through which God reveals himself: the Book of Scripture and the Book of Nature. Both, they argued, are authored by God — meaning they cannot ultimately contradict. History shows the church's relationship with science has been more complex, and more interesting, than a simple story of conflict or harmony.

Case Study 1Historical record

The Galileo affair (1610–1633)

🔭
1633

Galileo's trial before the Roman Inquisition

Galileo Galilei's telescopic observations supported Copernican heliocentrism (the earth orbits the sun) over the traditional Ptolemaic geocentric model. The Catholic Church's Inquisition, citing passages like Joshua 10:13 ("the sun stood still") and Psalm 93:1 ("the world is firmly established; it cannot be moved") as scriptural support for geocentrism, tried Galileo for "vehement suspicion of heresy," forced a recantation, and placed him under house arrest for the remainder of his life. His works remained on the Catholic Church's Index of Forbidden Books until 1835.

A more complex historical picture

Historians note the Galileo affair was not purely a "faith vs. science" conflict — it was tangled with personal politics (Galileo's combative personality and his satirizing of Pope Urban VIII, previously a supporter, in a published dialogue), the specific Counter-Reformation context (the Church was especially sensitive to any perceived challenge to scriptural authority following the Protestant Reformation), and genuine remaining scientific uncertainties at the time (the stellar parallax predicted by heliocentrism wasn't actually observed until 1838, two centuries later, which gave geocentrism's defenders some genuine scientific cover, however mistaken in hindsight).

In 1992, Pope John Paul II formally acknowledged the Church's error in the Galileo case, expressing regret for "the sorrow that this affair still causes."

Case Study 2Historical record

Darwin and the Scopes Trial (1859–1925)

🧬
1859

On the Origin of Species

Charles Darwin's publication proposing evolution by natural selection produced an immediate and varied Christian response — ranging from outright rejection to relatively quick theological accommodation. Notably, many prominent 19th-century theologians, including B.B. Warfield (a principal architect of the modern doctrine of biblical inerrancy at Princeton Theological Seminary) and Asa Gray (Harvard botanist and devout Presbyterian, Darwin's chief American defender), found evolution compatible with orthodox Christian faith relatively early — a more nuanced initial reception than popular memory often suggests.

⚖️
1925

The Scopes "Monkey Trial"

Tennessee teacher John Scopes was prosecuted for violating a state law banning the teaching of evolution in public schools. The trial became a cultural spectacle, pitting famous attorney Clarence Darrow (defense) against three-time presidential candidate William Jennings Bryan (prosecution). Scopes was convicted (later overturned on a technicality), but the trial's press coverage, especially H.L. Mencken's mocking commentary, significantly damaged fundamentalism's public reputation and contributed to a broader cultural narrative of inevitable conflict between faith and science.

Case Study 3Historical record

An unexpected reversal: the Big Bang and a Catholic priest

💥
1927

Georges Lemaître's "primeval atom" hypothesis

The Big Bang theory — that the universe began from an extremely hot, dense initial state and has been expanding ever since — was first proposed not by a secular scientist seeking to undermine religion, but by Georges Lemaître, a Belgian Roman Catholic priest and physicist, in 1927. Strikingly, some prominent atheist scientists of the era, including Fred Hoyle (who coined the dismissive term "Big Bang" and championed a rival "Steady State" theory in which the universe had no beginning), initially resisted the theory partly because a universe with a beginning seemed uncomfortably compatible with a Genesis-style creation event. This is a notable historical reversal of the popular "religion resists science" narrative: in this case, some scientists resisted a theory partly because of its perceived theological implications.

Pope Pius XII enthusiastically (perhaps overly so, in Lemaître's own more cautious view as a careful scientist) embraced the Big Bang in a 1951 address as scientific confirmation of biblical creation — a connection Lemaître himself, wary of conflating physics and theology too directly, gently discouraged the Pope from overstating.

Scholarly Perspectives

What historians of science and religion say

The popular "warfare narrative" between science and religion — most associated with two influential 19th-century books, John William Draper's History of the Conflict Between Religion and Science (1874) and Andrew Dickson White's A History of the Warfare of Science with Theology in Christendom (1896) — is now considered by professional historians of science to be a substantially oversimplified and in places historically inaccurate account that nonetheless became deeply embedded in popular culture.

Ronald NumbersUniversity of Wisconsin–Madison; historian of science; editor of Galileo Goes to Jail and Other Myths about Science and Religion (2009)

Far from being inherently opposed, the historical rise of modern science in 16th–17th century Europe was substantially enabled by a Christian theological framework — the conviction that a rational Creator had made an orderly, law-governed, and therefore intelligible universe worth carefully studying.

Peter HarrisonUniversity of Queensland; historian of science and religion; author of The Territories of Science and Religion (2015)

Each historical faith-science controversy — Galileo, Darwin, the Big Bang — ultimately required theology to refine, not abandon, its reading of Scripture. The consistent pattern is not capitulation but clarification: a more careful, contextually sensitive reading of the biblical text, not a wholesale retreat from biblical authority.

Alister McGrathOxford University; theologian and former scientist; author of Science and Religion: A New Introduction (2010)

The history of these conflicts should make the church humble, not capitulating. We have been wrong about specific scientific conclusions before — but we have also been right to hold certain theological commitments steady even under significant cultural and scientific pressure. Discerning which is which, in any given live controversy, takes real wisdom.

John LennoxOxford University; mathematician and Christian apologist

Closing Reflection

What this history teaches

"The heavens declare the glory of God; the skies proclaim the work of his hands."Psalm 19:1

The recurring historical pattern is instructive: in Galileo's case, the church's specific reading of Scripture needed correction, and science was right. In some 19th- and 20th-century debates about the universe's origin, certain scientists resisted evidence (the Big Bang) partly for non-scientific philosophical reasons. Neither "side" of the faith-science conversation has a monopoly on humility or on being wrong — both have needed correction at different moments, and both have also been right when it mattered. A prior commitment to intellectual humility, on both sides, is what each historical episode in this topic ultimately commends.

See: Numbers, Galileo Goes to Jail and Other Myths about Science and Religion (2009); Harrison, The Territories of Science and Religion (2015); McGrath, Science and Religion: A New Introduction (2010).
