IntroductionHigh tension

What is hell — and who goes there?

Hell is among the most contested doctrines in contemporary Christianity. The traditional view — eternal conscious torment — has been held by most Christians for most of church history, but it has always had dissenters, and the conversation has only intensified.

Original LanguageGreek & Hebrew study

The four biblical words translated "hell"

שְׁאוֹלSheolHebrew

The shadowy realm of the dead — not specifically a place of punishment

The Old Testament's primary term for the afterlife destination of all the dead, righteous and wicked alike (e.g., Jacob expects to go to Sheol in mourning, Genesis 37:35). It denotes the grave or a shadowy underworld existence, not a place of fiery torment — that later, more developed concept emerges primarily in the intertestamental and New Testament periods.

ᾅδηςHadēsGreek

The Greek equivalent of Sheol; borrowed from Greek mythology

The Septuagint translates Sheol as Hadēs, borrowing the name of the Greek underworld god/realm. In the New Testament it generally functions similarly to Sheol — the realm of the dead generally — though in Luke 16:23 (the parable of the rich man and Lazarus) it appears to include conscious torment for the wicked specifically.

γέενναGeenna (Gehenna)Greek

Jesus's primary word for hell — named after a real, physical valley

Gehenna is a Greek transliteration of the Hebrew Ge Hinnom ("Valley of Hinnom"), a real ravine just outside Jerusalem's walls. In the Old Testament it was the site of child sacrifice to the god Molech (2 Kings 23:10; Jeremiah 7:31) and by the first century was associated with continually burning refuse and decay. Jesus uses this evocative, concrete geographic image — a smoldering garbage dump where "the worm does not die" (Mark 9:48, echoing Isaiah 66:24) — as his primary metaphor for final judgment. Whether Jesus intended Gehenna as a literal future place or as a vivid, culturally available image for ultimate destruction/judgment is itself debated.

ΤάρταροςTartaros (Tartarus)Greek

Used only once (2 Peter 2:4) — borrowed from Greek mythology's deepest abyss

2 Peter 2:4 uses this term (in verb form, "cast into Tartarus") specifically for the place of imprisonment for fallen angels awaiting judgment — borrowing, similarly to Hadēs, a term from Greek mythological cosmology (Tartarus being the deepest abyss below even Hades in Greek myth) and applying it within a biblical theological framework.

The Central Word DebateGreek study

What does "eternal" actually mean?

αἰώνιοςaiōniosGreek

"Of the age" / "age-long" — duration debated

This is the Greek word behind "eternal" in passages like Matthew 25:46 ("eternal punishment"). Its root, aiōn ("age"), more directly denotes a long period or "age" than strict philosophical infinity. Annihilationist scholars argue aiōnios describes the quality or epoch of the coming age rather than necessarily unending individual duration — pointing to its use elsewhere (e.g., Jude 7's "eternal fire" judging Sodom, a fire that is obviously not still burning today, suggesting the word can describe a complete, decisive judgment rather than literally endless ongoing duration). Traditionalist scholars respond that Matthew 25:46 places "eternal punishment" in direct grammatical parallel with "eternal life" — if one is unending, consistent translation requires the other to be unending as well.

Scholarly Perspectives

What modern theologians say

The cumulative weight of New Testament destruction language — perish, destroy, death, ruin — most naturally suggests the wicked ultimately cease to exist, rather than suffering consciously forever. I came to this conclusion not lightly, but through sustained exegesis, and I believe it is the more biblically defensible position, even though it remains a minority view among evangelicals.

John StottInfluential evangelical leader; All Souls Church, London; in Evangelical Essentials (1988)

Universalism is not a denial of judgment but a confidence that judgment itself is ultimately purifying and restorative rather than endlessly retributive — consistent with a God whose love, as 1 Corinthians 13 says, "never fails."

Thomas TalbottWillamette University; author of The Inescapable Love of God (1999)

Eternal conscious torment remains the most exegetically and historically defensible position, even though it is the hardest doctrine in all of Christian theology to hold without flinching. The seriousness of sin against an infinitely worthy God is part of what's at stake in taking the traditional view seriously.

D.A. CarsonTrinity Evangelical Divinity School

I don't think Scripture gives us a fully systematic, airtight doctrine of hell's precise nature. We are given enough to take judgment with absolute seriousness, and perhaps not quite enough to resolve every detail with full confidence.

Christopher MorganCalifornia Baptist University; co-editor of Hell Under Fire (2004)

Christian Positions

The four major views

Eternal Conscious Torment

The lost suffer consciously forever

Matthew 25:46 parallels "eternal punishment" with "eternal life" — consistent translation requires both to be unending. Revelation 14:11 speaks explicitly of unending torment. Roman Catholicism, Eastern Orthodoxy (with nuance), most traditional Protestant theology.

Annihilationism / Conditional Immortality

The unsaved are destroyed, not tormented forever

Biblical language overwhelmingly describes destruction and perishing, not eternal torment. Humans are not inherently immortal; immortality is a gift given only to the redeemed. John Stott, Clark Pinnock, Edward Fudge; majority view among Seventh-day Adventists; growing among evangelicals.

Christian Universalism

God will ultimately reconcile all people

1 Timothy 2:4 ("God wants all people to be saved"), Colossians 1:20 ("reconciling all things"). Salvation is not universal immediately or without judgment, but eventually. Origen, Gregory of Nyssa, George MacDonald; a growing "evangelical universalist" movement today.

Purgatory (Catholic/some Orthodox/Anglican)

A process of purification precedes final union with God

Souls who die in grace but imperfectly purified undergo cleansing before heaven — not a "second chance," but completion of sanctification already begun in life. Hell remains for the definitively impenitent.

See: Fudge, The Fire That Consumes (annihilationism); Parry, The Evangelical Universalist (universalism); Morgan & Peterson, Hell Under Fire (traditional).
