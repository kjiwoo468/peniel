Introduction

What does the Bible actually teach about money?

From "blessed are the poor" to televangelists promising financial breakthrough for seed-faith donations, Christian teaching on wealth spans an enormous range. This topic examines the key texts, the Jubilee economic vision, and the controversial prosperity gospel movement.

Key TextGreek study

"Easier for a camel to go through the eye of a needle"

"It is easier for a camel to go through the eye of a needle than for someone who is rich to enter the kingdom of God."Matthew 19:24; Mark 10:25; Luke 18:25
κάμηλονkamēlonGreek

Camel (the literal, large desert animal)

A popular but unfounded legend claims "the eye of the needle" referred to a small gate in Jerusalem's walls that a camel could only pass through by kneeling and being unloaded — supposedly softening Jesus's statement into "difficult but achievable." No archaeological or textual evidence supports the existence of such a gate; the legend appears to originate in a medieval sermon and has no basis in the actual Greek text or first-century Jerusalem topography. The Greek word is the ordinary word for the literal animal, and "eye of a needle" (Greek trēma rhaphidos) is the ordinary word for a literal sewing needle. Jesus's statement is intentionally, hyperbolically impossible — making the disciples' shocked response ("Who then can be saved?" — Matthew 19:25) make complete narrative sense.

Jesus's own answer — "With man this is impossible, but with God all things are possible" (Matthew 19:26) — clarifies that he is not offering practical financial advice but making a theological point about the impossibility of anyone, rich or poor, earning entry into the kingdom apart from grace.

Old Testament Vision

The Year of Jubilee

"Consecrate the fiftieth year and proclaim liberty throughout the land to all its inhabitants. It shall be a jubilee for you; each of you is to return to your family property and to your own clan."Leviticus 25:10

Every 50th year, Israelite law mandated: enslaved Israelites freed, ancestral land returned to original families regardless of intervening sale, and agricultural debts effectively cancelled. This was a radical built-in mechanism against permanent generational wealth concentration and inherited poverty — though there is no extra-biblical historical evidence the Jubilee was ever actually, fully implemented in ancient Israel's history, and some scholars consider it primarily an idealized legal-theological vision rather than enforced practice.

Jesus's inaugural sermon in Luke 4:18–19 ("He has sent me to proclaim freedom for the prisoners... to set the oppressed free, to proclaim the year of the Lord's favor") directly echoes Jubilee language from Isaiah 61, leading many scholars to read Jesus's entire ministry as a kind of enacted, spiritualized Jubilee.

Modern ControversyWidely criticized

"Seed faith" and the health-and-wealth movement

The prosperity gospel — the teaching that faith, positive confession, and financial giving to ministries will produce health and material wealth — is one of the most theologically controversial movements in global Christianity, and one of the fastest-growing, especially in parts of Africa, Latin America, and Asia.

Key prosperity gospel proof texts and their challenges

  • 3 John 2 — "I pray that you may prosper in all things and be in health, just as your soul prospers" (NKJV) is widely cited, but most scholars note this is a conventional ancient letter-opening greeting (similar formulas appear in secular papyrus letters of the era), not a doctrinal promise of material wealth.
  • Malachi 3:10 ("test me... and see if I will not throw open the floodgates of heaven") is cited for "seed faith" giving, but addresses corporate Israelite tithing under the Old Covenant temple system, not individual prosperity-ministry donations.
  • Deuteronomy 8:18 ("he gives you the ability to produce wealth") is part of a chapter warning against pride in wealth and forgetting God — arguably the opposite point prosperity teachers draw from it.

The prosperity gospel essentially inverts the theology of the cross. Where the New Testament consistently links faithfulness with the possibility of suffering, persecution, and loss, the prosperity gospel promises that real faith eliminates suffering — a claim flatly contradicted by Paul's own life and explicit teaching (2 Corinthians 11:23–27; Philippians 4:12).

Kate BowlerDuke Divinity School; author of Blessed: A History of the American Prosperity Gospel (2013)

The global growth of prosperity teaching, especially in contexts of severe poverty, deserves serious theological and pastoral engagement rather than dismissal — it speaks to real hope and real desperation, even where its theology requires significant correction.

Adeyemi AdewoleAfrican theologian; writer on prosperity theology in the Global South

Christian Positions

Major Christian views on wealth

Prosperity Theology

Faith and giving produce material blessing

God wants believers to prosper financially and physically; poverty and sickness can reflect insufficient faith or unconfessed sin, and "seed" giving activates God's financial blessing. Associated with Kenneth Copeland, Joel Osteen, Creflo Dollar; widely criticized by mainstream evangelical, Catholic, and Orthodox theologians as a distortion of the gospel.

Stewardship Theology

Wealth is a trust to be managed faithfully, not a guaranteed reward

God owns everything (Psalm 24:1); believers are stewards, not owners, called to generosity, contentment, and wise management. Wealth is neither inherently sinful nor a sign of righteousness. The mainstream evangelical and broad Protestant consensus.

Liberation Theology

God has a preferential option for the poor

Scripture's consistent concern for the poor, oppressed, and marginalized requires the church to actively work for economic justice and structural change, not merely individual charity. Associated with Gustavo Gutiérrez, James Cone, and broader Catholic social teaching.

Simplicity / Radical Discipleship

Christians are called to material simplicity and sacrificial generosity

Drawing on Jesus's call to the rich young ruler (Mark 10:21) and the early church's communal sharing (Acts 2:44–45), this view calls for intentional simplicity and direct, sacrificial generosity as core to Christian discipleship. Associated with the Anabaptist tradition, Shane Claiborne, Ronald Sider.
