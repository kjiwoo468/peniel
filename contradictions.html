Section 1

Introduction

Does the Bible contradict itself?

Critics and honest believers alike point to passages that seem to say different things about the same event. Dismissing these as non-issues can feel dishonest; treating them as fatal can feel like giving up too soon. This topic walks through the most commonly cited examples with real textual-critical and historical analysis.

Case Study 1Moderate tension

How did Judas die?

TextAccount
Matthew 27:5"So Judas threw the money into the temple and left. Then he went away and hanged himself."
Acts 1:18"With the payment he received for his wickedness, Judas bought a field; there he fell headlong, his body burst open and all his intestines spilled out."

The difficulty

Matthew has Judas hang himself; the field is bought by the chief priests with his returned money (Matthew 27:6–8). Acts has Judas himself buying the field, and dying by a fall that bursts his body open — no mention of hanging.

Scholarly responses

Harmonization: A traditional explanation suggests Judas hanged himself, the rope or branch broke or decayed, and his body fell and burst open — accounting for both descriptions. Critics note this requires combining details neither text actually states.

Different traditions: Many scholars (including evangelical ones) conclude that Matthew and Luke (author of Acts) preserve two independent early Christian traditions about Judas's death and the field's purchase, reflecting the messy way oral history develops even about significant events, without requiring either to be "wrong" in a moralized sense — both express the same underlying conviction: Judas met a shameful, divinely-judged end.

Linguistic note: the Greek word in Matthew, apēnxato, unambiguously means "hanged himself." Acts uses prēnēs genomenos elakēsen mesos, a graphic and unusual phrase that some scholars argue could describe a body left hanging until it decomposed and fell — which would actually harmonize the accounts more naturally than "falling" from a standing position.

Case Study 2Moderate tension

David's census: how many soldiers?

TextIsraelJudahWho incited David?
2 Samuel 24:9800,000500,000The LORD (2 Sam 24:1)
1 Chronicles 21:51,100,000470,000Satan (1 Chr 21:1)

A textual transmission explanation

Ancient Hebrew used letters as numerals. A single miscopied letter could shift a number by hundreds of thousands. Most textual scholars regard the numerical discrepancy as a copying error introduced during the centuries of hand-transmission, not an error in the original autographs. The more theologically striking discrepancy — whether God or Satan incited David — is usually explained as complementary: in Hebrew thought, God's sovereign permission and Satan's active temptation are not mutually exclusive (compare Job 1–2, where Satan acts only within God's permission).

What modern scholarship adds

Many scholars (e.g., contributors to the NIV Study Bible notes and the New Bible Commentary) suggest "thousand" (Hebrew 'eleph) may sometimes denote a military unit rather than a literal count of 1,000 men — which would dramatically reduce the totals and bring the two accounts closer together, though this remains debated among Hebrew specialists.

Case Study 3Moderate tension

Who went to the tomb, and what did they see?

GospelWho visitedWho/what they encountered
Matthew 28:1–7Mary Magdalene, "the other Mary"One angel, sitting on the stone
Mark 16:1–5Mary Magdalene, Mary mother of James, SalomeOne young man, inside the tomb
Luke 24:1–4Several women including Mary Magdalene, Joanna, Mary mother of JamesTwo men in dazzling clothes
John 20:1–12Mary Magdalene (alone, initially)Two angels, after she alerts Peter and John

Honest assessment

These differences are real and not trivially resolved. Apologists have proposed harmonizations (multiple groups of women visiting at different times; one angel "speaking" while two are present). Critics argue these harmonizations create a composite story none of the Gospel writers actually tell. The core facts agreed across all four — the tomb was empty, women were the first witnesses, angelic beings were present, Jesus appeared alive afterward — are considered by many historians (including skeptical ones) to be early and well-attested, even while the precise sequence of events differs by account.

What scholars say about this kind of variation

The differences between the resurrection accounts are exactly what we'd expect from independent, honest testimony to a chaotic, emotionally overwhelming event — not from a single coordinated fabrication. Court testimony from real witnesses to traumatic events regularly looks like this.

N.T. WrightFormer Bishop of Durham; author of The Resurrection of the Son of God (2003)

The Gospel writers were not attempting twenty-first-century court-transcript accuracy. They were writing theological narrative within the conventions of ancient biography, which permitted compression, selection, and rearrangement that would not be considered "error" by ancient standards.

Richard BauckhamUniversity of St Andrews; author of Jesus and the Eyewitnesses (2006)

Positions

Five ways Christians read apparent contradictions

Harmonization

The accounts can be reconciled

Careful study shows many apparent contradictions dissolve under close reading — different details from the same event, not incompatible claims. Requires intellectual work but not dismissing any text.

Genre & Authorial Intent

Ancient writing followed different conventions

Ancient biography and history permitted compression, paraphrase, and selective emphasis that modern readers mistake for error. The Gospel authors wrote theological portraits, not court transcripts.

Textual Transmission

Some errors entered during copying

The autographs were without error; some discrepancies (like the census numbers) likely reflect scribal transmission errors over centuries of hand-copying, recoverable through textual criticism.

Productive Multiplicity

Differences reflect the richness of witness

The four Gospels were never meant to be harmonized into one narrative. Preserving four distinct, sometimes divergent accounts is itself a form of truth-telling about a complex reality.

Honest Acknowledgment

Some discrepancies remain genuinely unresolved

Intellectual honesty requires admitting that not every apparent contradiction has a fully satisfying resolution — and that this need not be theologically catastrophic.
