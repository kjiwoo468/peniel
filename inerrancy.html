Biblical Inerrancy

Section 1

Introduction

Is the Bible without error — and in what sense?

Few questions divide evangelical Christianity more sharply. "Inerrancy" — the claim that the Bible contains no errors — has been a litmus test of orthodoxy in some circles and a source of pastoral difficulty in others. Yet the question is more nuanced than either side often acknowledges.

Why this question is unavoidable

  • The Bible contains passages that give different numbers for the same events — compare 2 Samuel 24:9 (800,000 warriors in Israel) with 1 Chronicles 21:5 (1,100,000). The gap is 300,000 soldiers.
  • Scientific claims in Scripture — the sun stopping (Joshua 10:13), the mustard seed as "the smallest of all seeds" (Matthew 13:32), a global flood covering all mountains — conflict with modern science.
  • No original manuscripts (autographs) survive. All we have are copies of copies, containing thousands of textual variants.
  • Yet 2 Timothy 3:16 says "all Scripture is God-breathed." What follows from that for questions of accuracy?

Original LanguageGreek Study

What does "God-breathed" actually mean?

The entire doctrine of inerrancy rests on one Greek word in 2 Timothy 3:16.

θεόπνευστος theopneustos Greek

Literally: "God-breathed" or "breathed out by God"

This word appears only once in the entire New Testament — in 2 Timothy 3:16. It is almost certainly a word Paul coined himself, compounding theos (God) and pneustos (from pneo, to breathe). The word describes what Scripture is, not how it was produced. Key debate: does it mean God breathed into the text (so that the writers were divinely assisted) or that God breathed out the words (so that the very words originate from God)?

γραφή graphē Greek

Scripture / Writing

The word translated "Scripture" in 2 Timothy 3:16. In Paul's time, graphē referred to the Hebrew Scriptures (the Old Testament). Whether Paul intended his own letters — or any later Christian writings — to fall under this umbrella is itself a question of interpretation.

Translation comparison — 2 Timothy 3:16

VersionRendering of key phraseTheological note
KJV (1611)"All scripture is given by inspiration of God""Inspiration" softens the "breathed-out" force; implies God breathed something in
ESV (2001)"All Scripture is breathed out by God"Deliberately recovers the "breathed out" direction; favored by inerrantists
NIV (2011)"All Scripture is God-breathed"Transliterates rather than translates; leaves directionality open
NRSV (1989)"All scripture is inspired by God"Returns to "inspired" language; favored by mainline traditions

Note: the KJV's "inspiration" comes via the Latin inspiratio (Jerome's Vulgate, c. 400 AD) which emphasized God breathing into the text. The original Greek direction may in fact be the opposite.

Textual HistoryManuscripts

What we mean when we say "the Bible"

Inerrancy is usually claimed for the autographs — the original manuscripts. But no autographs survive. This raises a critical question: inerrancy of a document we no longer possess.

📜
c. 250 BC — 4th Century AD

The Manuscript Tradition

We possess approximately 5,800 Greek New Testament manuscripts, ranging from tiny fragments to complete Bibles. No two are identical. Scholars estimate 200,000–400,000 textual variants exist across the manuscript tradition, the vast majority trivial (spelling, word order), but some theologically significant. The Old Testament is attested primarily by the Masoretic Text (9th–10th century AD) and the Septuagint (3rd century BC), which sometimes differ substantially.

Key textual fact

The famous passage about the woman caught in adultery (John 7:53–8:11) is absent from the earliest and best Greek manuscripts (Papyrus 66, Papyrus 75, Codex Sinaiticus, Codex Vaticanus). It appears to have been added centuries later. Most modern translations include it with a footnote noting this.

The "Wicked Bible" of 1631

A famous misprint: London printers Robert Barker and Martin Lucas accidentally omitted the word "not" from Exodus 20:14, making the Seventh Commandment read: "Thou shalt commit adultery." King Charles I ordered the Bibles recalled; the printers were fined £300 (roughly £50,000 today). About 11 copies survive.

Scholarly Perspectives

What modern scholars and theologians say

Inerrancy of the autographs is an inference from the character of God, not directly a claim of Scripture itself. The Bible claims to be God's Word; it does not define what that means for issues of factual accuracy.

Bruce MetzgerProfessor of New Testament Language and Literature, Princeton Theological Seminary; leading New Testament textual critic

The Chicago Statement says the Bible is "without error or fault in all its teaching" and that this "applies to the whole of Scripture and to all its parts." I think this is right — but it requires us to read Scripture on its own terms, not ours. Ancient historiography did not require the standards of accuracy modern readers assume.

J.I. PackerRegent College; primary drafter of the Chicago Statement on Biblical Inerrancy (1978)

To call the Bible "inerrant" is to use a modern concept to describe an ancient text, and inevitably to misread both. The Bible is not the kind of book that can be inerrant in the modern sense — and recognizing that is not a retreat from its authority, but a more honest engagement with it.

Peter EnnsBiblical scholar; former Westminster Theological Seminary; author of Inspiration and Incarnation (2005)

I affirm the inerrancy of Scripture while acknowledging that we don't possess the inerrant autographs and that the process of textual transmission has introduced errors into the copies. The doctrine functions more as an affirmation of trust than a claim about every detail.

Kenton SparksEastern University; author of God's Word in Human Words (2008)

Christian Positions

The four major views

Full / Strict Inerrancy

The Bible is without error in all that it affirms

Scripture is truthful in every area it addresses — history, science, and theology. Apparent contradictions reflect our misunderstanding. This is the position of the Chicago Statement on Biblical Inerrancy (1978), the Southern Baptist Convention, and most conservative evangelical institutions. Proponents: Wayne Grudem, John MacArthur, Al Mohler.

Biblical Infallibility

Reliable in matters of faith and salvation

Scripture is without error in what it intends to teach — the gospel, doctrine, ethics, salvation — but does not claim to be a textbook of modern science or history. Minor factual discrepancies do not undermine biblical authority. Common in Anglican, mainline Protestant, and many evangelical traditions. Proponents: John Stott, N.T. Wright (cautiously).

Functional Inspiration

Scripture reliably accomplishes God's purposes

The Bible is fully inspired and accomplishes everything God intends, but must be read through its ancient genres, literary conventions, and human authorship. "Incarnational analogy": as Jesus was fully human and fully divine, Scripture is fully human (subject to human limitations) and fully divine (authoritative in purpose). Proponents: Peter Enns, Kenton Sparks.

Catholic / Orthodox View

Scripture and Tradition interpreted together by the Church

The Bible is inspired and without error in what it teaches, but correct interpretation requires the Church's living Tradition and Magisterium (Catholic) or the Holy Tradition and ecumenical councils (Orthodox). The Church produced the Bible; it alone can authoritatively interpret it.

Reflection

Sitting with the question

"For my thoughts are not your thoughts, neither are your ways my ways," declares the Lord. "As the heavens are higher than the earth, so are my ways higher than your ways and my thoughts than your thoughts."Isaiah 55:8–9

One consistent theme in this debate: our expectation that divine communication must look a certain way (error-free by modern standards) may say more about us than about God. The incarnation itself — God entering human limitation — may be the best analogy for how Scripture works: fully God's word, fully in human words.
