Section 1

Introduction

Who decided what belongs in the Bible?

The 66 books in the Protestant Bible — or 73 in the Catholic Bible, or 81 in the Ethiopian Orthodox Bible — did not fall from the sky in their current form. They were assembled over centuries by human beings making judgment calls. If the canon is a human product, how can we trust it as God's Word?

HistoryHistorical record

How the Hebrew canon formed

📜
c. 1200–165 BC

The books were written over roughly a millennium

From the earliest poetic fragments (Judges 5, the "Song of Deborah," among the oldest Hebrew texts) to Daniel (commonly dated by critical scholars to the 2nd century BC), the Hebrew Bible's composition spans an enormous period.

🏛️
c. 90 AD

The "Council" of Jamnia (Yavneh)

Long described as the moment the Hebrew canon was formally closed, modern scholarship is more cautious: Jamnia was a rabbinic academy, not a council with binding authority, and the discussions there (recorded in the Mishnah) concerned a few disputed books (Ecclesiastes, Song of Songs) whose status was already largely settled in practice.

🏺
Discovered 1947–1956

The Dead Sea Scrolls

Found at Qumran, these scrolls (dated roughly 250 BC–70 AD) include fragments of every Old Testament book except Esther, plus numerous non-canonical works (Enoch, Jubilees, the Community Rule). Their existence confirms that a recognizable body of authoritative Hebrew Scripture existed well before the Christian era — but also shows other religious texts circulating alongside it without a fixed boundary.

The Septuagint

The Greek translation of the Hebrew Scriptures (3rd–2nd century BC), used by most New Testament authors when quoting the Old Testament, included additional books — Tobit, Judith, 1 & 2 Maccabees, Wisdom, Sirach, Baruch — that Protestant Bibles exclude but Catholic and Orthodox Bibles retain as the "Deuterocanon" (Catholic) or simply canonical (Orthodox).

HistoryHistorical record

How the 27-book New Testament formed

✉️
c. 140 AD

Marcion's canon

The heretic Marcion proposed the first known "New Testament" canon — a truncated Luke and ten Pauline letters, explicitly excluding the Old Testament and anything "too Jewish." His radical pruning is widely seen as forcing the wider church to begin articulating its own canon in response.

📋
c. 170–200 AD

The Muratorian Fragment

The earliest known list of New Testament books, surviving in a damaged 7th-century Latin copy. It includes most of our 27 books but lists Hebrews, James, and 1 & 2 Peter as absent or disputed, and includes the now-noncanonical Apocalypse of Peter as disputed-but-read.

📨
367 AD

Athanasius's Easter Letter

The earliest surviving document to list exactly our current 27 New Testament books as canonical, with no more and no fewer — a major milestone, though not yet binding on the wider church.

⛪
393 & 397 AD

Councils of Hippo and Carthage

Regional North African councils, with Augustine's significant influence, formally ratified the 27-book New Testament. This was confirmed (not newly decided) by later ecumenical recognition — these councils affirmed what was already broad consensus rather than imposing a novel decision.

Books that were disputed for centuries

Revelation, Hebrews, James, Jude, 2 Peter, 2 and 3 John were all questioned by significant church figures well into the 4th century. Eusebius of Caesarea (c. 325 AD) categorized New Testament books as "recognized," "disputed," and "spurious" — placing James, Jude, 2 Peter, and 2–3 John in the "disputed" category.

Books that nearly made it in

The Shepherd of Hermas, the Epistle of Barnabas, and 1 Clement were treated as authoritative Scripture by significant portions of the early church (they appear in Codex Sinaiticus, a 4th-century complete Bible) before ultimately being excluded.

Scholarly Perspectives

What modern scholars say

The canon was not imposed from above by a single decisive council, but emerged gradually through the converging judgment of many churches over centuries — a process more like discernment than decree.

Bruce MetzgerPrinceton Theological Seminary; author of The Canon of the New Testament (1987)

Lists like the Muratorian Fragment show real diversity and real disagreement in the early centuries. The clean story of an obviously closed, universally agreed canon from the beginning does not match the historical evidence.

Lee Martin McDonaldAuthor of The Biblical Canon: Its Origin, Transmission, and Authority (2006)

The criteria the early church actually used — apostolic origin, widespread use in worship, theological consistency with the "rule of faith" — were reasonable and theologically serious, even if applied imperfectly and over a long period of time.

F.F. BruceUniversity of Manchester; author of The Canon of Scripture (1988)

Christian Positions

How traditions account for canon formation

Spirit-Led Recognition

The Spirit guided the church to recognize Scripture

God superintended canonization through the Spirit working in the church across centuries. The church did not create Scripture by deciding it was canonical — it recognized what already was. The Protestant consensus.

Catholic / Orthodox View

The Church's authority guarantees the canon

Because the Church produced and preserved the Bible, the Magisterium (Catholic) or Holy Tradition (Orthodox) authoritatively determines and interprets the canon — avoiding the circularity of using the Bible to prove the Bible.

Historical-Critical

The canon reflects early Christian politics and circumstance

Canonization was shaped by theological controversy (Marcion forced a response), regional church politics, and which texts survived and circulated. Honesty requires acknowledging this was a genuinely human, contingent process.

Self-Attesting Scripture

Scripture bears witness to its own authority

Reformed theology (following Calvin) holds that canonical books possess a self-authenticating quality the Spirit uses to produce conviction in believers — the "internal testimony of the Holy Spirit."
