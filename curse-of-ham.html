Race & the Curse of Ham

← History & Justice

Section 1

IntroductionHigh tension

The text that was weaponized for centuries

Genesis 9 records Noah cursing his grandson Canaan after Noah's son Ham sees him naked. For several centuries, European and American theologians used this text to argue Black African people were divinely cursed to be enslaved. This interpretation was mainstream in antebellum America. It is among the most egregious misreadings in the history of biblical interpretation — and understanding exactly how and why it was wrong is essential.

The TextHebrew study

What Genesis 9 actually says

"When Noah awoke from his wine and found out what his youngest son had done to him, he said: 'Cursed be Canaan! The lowest of slaves will he be to his brothers.'"Genesis 9:24–25
כְּנַעַןKena'anHebrew

Canaan — a specific person/nation, not a race or skin color

The curse falls explicitly and exclusively on Canaan, one of Ham's four sons (Genesis 10:6) — not on Ham himself, and not on Ham's other sons. The Canaanites are the people who would later inhabit the land Israel was to possess. No word for skin color appears anywhere in this passage. The identification of this curse with Black African people requires several unsupported interpretive leaps: (1) equating Ham generally with Africa, (2) ignoring that the curse falls on Canaan specifically, not Ham, and (3) inserting race/color into a text that never mentions it.

Ham's actual genealogy (Genesis 10:6)

Ham's four sons are Cush (associated with ancient Nubia/Ethiopia), Mizraim (Egypt), Put (likely Libya), and Canaan (the Levant/Palestine region — not Africa at all). The peoples most closely associated with Black Africa in the biblical world — the Cushites — descend from Ham's son Cush, who receives no curse whatsoever. The only cursed line, Canaan, settled in the Levant, geographically nowhere near sub-Saharan Africa.

Historical MisuseHistorical record

The history of a catastrophic misinterpretation

📚
Medieval period

Early seeds in rabbinic and Islamic commentary

Some early medieval Jewish and Islamic commentators began associating Ham's descendants with dark skin, in texts disconnected from later American slavery debates — an important but separate strand of the broader historical association, predating the transatlantic slave trade by centuries.

⛵
15th–16th century

Justifying the transatlantic slave trade

As European powers began the transatlantic slave trade, theologians and slave traders increasingly invoked the "Curse of Ham" (conflating Ham with Canaan, and Canaan's curse with dark skin and perpetual servitude) as religious cover for an already-profitable economic enterprise.

🇺🇸
18th–19th century, American South

Mainstream pro-slavery theology

The "Curse of Ham" became a standard, widely preached justification in pro-slavery American Christianity. Prominent Southern theologians, including Presbyterian minister and seminary professor Robert Lewis Dabney, taught it as established doctrine. It was not a fringe view — it was mainstream Southern pulpit teaching for generations.

A telling counter-text the slaveholders ignored

Psalm 68:31 states: "Envoys will come from Egypt; Cush will submit herself to God" — depicting Cush (associated with ancient Nubia/Ethiopia, Black Africa) positively, as a nation that will worship the true God. The Ethiopian eunuch of Acts 8, among the first Gentile converts, is treated by the text with full dignity and respect. The "Curse of Ham" reading required ignoring or suppressing this wider, more positive African presence throughout Scripture.

Scholarly Perspectives

What modern scholars say

The "Curse of Ham" stands as one of history's clearest examples of eisegesis driven by economic interest — reading meaning into a text that simply is not there, in order to justify a conclusion already reached for other reasons.

Stephen HaynesRhodes College; author of Noah's Curse: The Biblical Justification of American Slavery (2002)

Africa and African peoples occupy a significant, often overlooked, and largely positive place across the biblical narrative — from Moses's Cushite wife (Numbers 12:1) to Simon of Cyrene carrying Jesus's cross (Mark 15:21) to the flourishing North African church that produced Augustine, Tertullian, and Origen. The "Curse of Ham" tradition required suppressing this much larger and more positive witness.

Cain Hope FelderHoward University School of Divinity; editor of Stony the Road We Trod: African American Biblical Interpretation (1991)

Genesis 9's curse on Canaan most plausibly functions, in its ancient Israelite literary context, as a polemical justification for Israel's later conquest and subjugation of the Canaanite peoples specifically — not as a statement about race in any modern sense, which is an anachronistic category to read back into the ancient text at all.

David GoldenbergUniversity of Pennsylvania; author of The Curse of Ham: Race and Slavery in Early Judaism, Christianity, and Islam (2003)

The Broader Lesson

When interpreters get it catastrophically wrong

Hermeneutical Warning

Interested interpreters find what they want to find

This disaster is a case study in motivated reading. Slaveholders wanted theological sanction for a profitable system and built one through maximum creative license, ignoring the text's actual referent. The lesson: interpreters can be catastrophically wrong, especially with something material to gain.

Honest Confession

The church owes a specific debt of repentance

Many Southern denominations were founded specifically defending slavery using Scripture. The Southern Baptist Convention, founded in 1845 over this issue, formally apologized in 1995. Honest historical reckoning is an act of theological integrity.

See: Haynes, Noah's Curse (2002); Felder, Stony the Road We Trod (1991); Goldenberg, The Curse of Ham (2003).
