IntroductionModerate tension

Are miracles credible in a scientific age?

Christianity rests on a specific historical miracle claim: the bodily resurrection of Jesus. If naturalism is true — if the universe is a closed causal system with no room for divine intervention — Christianity is false. Are miracles intellectually credible for someone who takes modern science seriously?

The Philosophical Challenge

David Hume's case against believing in miracles

"A miracle is a violation of the laws of nature, and as a firm and unalterable experience has established these laws, the proof against a miracle... is as entire as any argument from experience can possibly be imagined."David Hume, An Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding, 1748

Hume's influential argument: because natural law is established by overwhelming, uniform experience, while testimony for any single miracle is comparatively limited, it will always be more rational to doubt the testimony (assume error, deception, or exaggeration) than to accept that a law of nature was actually violated.

The standard philosophical response

Many philosophers (including non-Christians) consider Hume's argument subtly circular: it assumes in advance that natural law is inviolable, then uses that assumption to dismiss any evidence that might suggest otherwise — meaning no possible amount of testimonial evidence could ever count, regardless of its actual strength. C.S. Lewis argued the real question is not "is the testimony reliable?" but "is the universe the kind of place where miracles are even possible?" — a metaphysical question Hume's argument doesn't actually settle, it presupposes.

Historical ArgumentHistorical evidence

The resurrection as a historical claim

⚰️

The empty tomb

Even ancient critics of Christianity (reflected in Matthew 28:11–15's polemic about guards being bribed to say the body was stolen) appear to concede the tomb was actually empty — they offer an alternative explanation for the emptiness rather than denying it, which many historians take as early indirect evidence the tomb's empty state was not seriously disputed even by opponents.

👥

Multiple, early, independent appearance traditions

Paul's list in 1 Corinthians 15:3–8 — widely dated by scholars across the theological spectrum to within just a few years of the crucifixion, making it among the earliest material in the entire New Testament — names specific individuals and groups (Peter, the Twelve, "more than 500 brothers and sisters at the same time, most of whom are still living," James, "all the apostles") as witnesses, explicitly inviting readers to verify with those still alive.

🔄

The radical transformation of skeptics

James (Jesus's biological brother, who according to the Gospels did not believe in him during his ministry — Mark 3:21; John 7:5) and Paul (an active persecutor of the early church) both became committed, sacrificial leaders of the movement they once rejected or attacked — a transformation historians find requires substantial explanation.

Skeptical historians offer alternative explanations: hallucination theories (though group hallucinations of this specific, consistent kind are psychologically unusual), theft of the body, or legendary development over time (though the early dating of 1 Corinthians 15 leaves little time for legend to develop). The debate continues to be argued seriously by professional historians on both sides.

Scholarly Perspectives

What historians and theologians say

The resurrection is, by the ordinary canons of historical inquiry applied to any other ancient historical claim, the best explanation for the convergence of the empty tomb, the early and multiply-attested appearance traditions, and the otherwise inexplicable transformation and willingness to suffer of the original disciples.

N.T. WrightAuthor of The Resurrection of the Son of God (2003), a 700-page historical case

As a historian, not a theologian, I conclude something happened that caused the early disciples to sincerely believe they had seen the risen Jesus. I do not believe historical method alone can adjudicate whether that experience was an actual bodily resurrection or some other event — that judgment ultimately involves philosophical and theological commitments beyond historical method's reach.

Bart EhrmanUniversity of North Carolina; agnostic New Testament historian

Miracles do not violate the laws of nature so much as they introduce a new factor into nature, which nature obediently complies with from that point forward. The laws of nature describe what happens given a fixed set of factors; a miracle is simply God adding a new factor to the equation.

C.S. LewisAuthor of Miracles (1947)

Healing and other miracle claims are reported with striking frequency across cultures and centuries, including from credible eyewitnesses and in contexts with reasonable documentation — and deserve serious historical examination, not automatic dismissal grounded only in prior philosophical assumptions about what's possible.

Craig KeenerAsbury Theological Seminary; author of Miracles: The Credibility of the New Testament Accounts (2011), a two-volume historical survey

Christian Positions

How Christians think about miracles

Classical Theism

Miracles are real but rare exceptions to normal natural law

God normally works through consistent natural laws he himself sustains; miracles are genuine, occasional direct interventions. A law-governed universe is itself the precondition for science to even be possible — far from undermining science, classical theism's God explains why nature is orderly enough to study.

Historical Apologetics

The resurrection is defensible on standard historical evidential grounds

Using the same historical methods applied to any other ancient claim, the convergence of evidence for the resurrection is best explained by its having actually occurred. N.T. Wright, William Lane Craig, Gary Habermas.

Demythologizing

Miracle stories point to spiritual truths, not necessarily physical events

Rudolf Bultmann's influential view: the New Testament's miraculous elements reflect an ancient mythological worldview that modern readers cannot accept literally. Theology's task is finding the existential truth beneath the miraculous "shell." A significant departure from historic Christian orthodoxy, which has consistently affirmed a bodily, historical resurrection.

Continuationism / Charismatic Theology

God actively continues to heal and intervene today

Miracles did not cease with the apostolic age (a view called "cessationism") but continue today through the ongoing work of the Holy Spirit. Global Pentecostalism — the fastest-growing form of Christianity worldwide — is largely built on this expectation. Roughly 600 million Pentecostal/charismatic Christians worldwide.

See: Lewis, Miracles (1947); Wright, The Resurrection of the Son of God (2003); Keener, Miracles (2011); Hume, An Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding (1748).
