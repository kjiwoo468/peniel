The Crusades & Christian Violence

← History & Justice

Section 1

IntroductionHigh tension

What actually happened in the Crusades — and why?

The Crusades are simultaneously invoked by critics of Christianity as evidence of its violent nature and by some Christians as misunderstood defensive wars unfairly blamed on the church. Both popular narratives distort a complex historical reality. Honest Christian engagement requires neither minimizing the violence nor accepting every contemporary charge without scrutiny.

Historical RecordHistorical evidence

What happened, and why

⚔️
1095

Pope Urban II's Call at Clermont

Pope Urban II called for armed pilgrimage to recover Jerusalem from Muslim rule, responding partly to requests from Byzantine Emperor Alexios I for military aid against Seljuk Turkish expansion. Urban promised spiritual benefits (remission of sins, not technically "salvation" in the crude popular form, though the theology was genuinely confused) for those who participated. The response from the European nobility was enormous and enthusiastic — driven by a mix of genuine religious piety, desire for land and wealth, and the new outlet this provided for the violence of European warrior culture.

🩸
1099

The Sack of Jerusalem

When Crusader armies took Jerusalem in July 1099, contemporary accounts describe a massacre of the city's Muslim and Jewish inhabitants. Chroniclers — including enthusiastic crusading supporters — record rivers of blood in the streets. Modern historians debate the scale (some accounts appear exaggerated for literary effect) but not the fundamental fact: a large-scale massacre of civilians occurred and was reported with pride by those who participated.

📜
1095–1291

The Major Crusades: Context and Complexity

Historians typically count seven to nine major Crusades, with numerous smaller expeditions. The context is often omitted from popular accounts: Muslim armies had conquered Christian-majority regions including North Africa, Spain, the Middle East, and parts of Europe in the preceding centuries; the Crusades emerged from a world where religious war was practiced and accepted by all parties, not a Christian innovation; and the Crusader States coexisted, often peacefully, with Muslim neighbors for generations. None of this justifies the atrocities committed, but it complicates a simple "Christianity equals violence" narrative.

🕍
1096

The Rhineland Massacres — Crusaders' First Victims Were Jewish

Before the First Crusade had even reached the Holy Land, crusading armies massacred Jewish communities throughout the Rhineland — Speyer, Worms, Mainz, and Cologne. The logic was what it had always been: if we are fighting Christ's enemies, why spare the enemies among us? This was formally condemned by Pope Urban and some bishops, but protection was inconsistently provided and the massacres proceeded. It represents Christian military violence against a population that had nothing to do with the ostensible cause of the Crusade.

Theological Roots

The ideas that made crusading possible

How "holy war" became thinkable for Christians

  • Augustine's just war theory had been developed for defensive state violence. By the 10th–11th century, the concept was being extended to offensive holy war — a significant theological shift that Augustine himself did not make and probably would not have endorsed.
  • The papacy's new political power: Gregory VII's 11th-century reforms vastly expanded papal authority, including over warfare. The idea that the pope could authorize military campaigns — and remit sins in exchange for participation — was new and theologically questionable even at the time.
  • Jerusalem's special status: The veneration of the Holy Land and desire to protect access to pilgrimage sites created a unique emotional and theological valence around Jerusalem that made armed recovery seem, to many, self-evidently just.
  • Bernard of Clairvaux's In Praise of the New Knighthood explicitly argued that killing "infidels" was not sin but meritorious, since the enemy was God's enemy. This represented a sharp departure from earlier Christian moral theology about killing.
Scholarly Perspectives

What historians say

The popular image of the Crusades as uniquely Christian aggression against a peaceful Islamic world distorts history in both directions. Muslim armies had conquered large Christian territories in the preceding centuries; the Crusades were a belated, brutal, and theologically confused response to an already violent world. That they involved genuine atrocity is undeniable; that they were simply "Christianity attacking Islam" without context is not accurate history.

Thomas MaddenSaint Louis University; author of The New Concise History of the Crusades (2005)

The Rhineland massacres of 1096 reveal what crusading theology, in its unguarded form, actually produced: violence against Jews who had been living in Christian Europe for centuries, driven by a logic that treated religious identity as the basis for who deserved protection. This is not medieval aberration — it is the crusading logic applied consistently.

David NirenbergUniversity of Chicago; author of Communities of Violence (1996)

Honest Christian reckoning with the Crusades requires neither adopting every contemporary anti-Christian accusation nor retreating into apologetics. The church authorized violence it should not have authorized, motivated violence with theology that distorted the Gospel, and produced massacres that its own contemporaries sometimes recognized as atrocities. That is enough to require genuine repentance, independent of how we evaluate the geopolitical context.

Jonathan Riley-SmithUniversity of Cambridge; leading Crusade historian; Catholic; The Crusades: A History (2005)

Christian Reckoning

What honest engagement looks like

Contextual Defense

The Crusades must be understood in their historical context

Medieval people lived in a world of endemic violence, where warfare was the primary tool of political order. The Crusades were not uniquely violent compared to contemporaries; they emerged from legitimate concerns about access to holy sites and protection of Christians; and many popular criticisms rest on anachronistic application of modern standards. Common in some apologetic and popular-Catholic accounts.

Honest Moral Reckoning

The church authorized genuine evil; historical context explains but does not excuse

Understanding why the Crusades happened as they did — political, economic, and theological factors — is essential for accurate history. But understanding is not excusing. The deliberate massacre of civilians, including the Rhineland Jewish communities, was condemned by the church's own moral standards at the time and requires acknowledgment, not apologetics. Most professional Christian historians; Pope John Paul II's 2000 Day of Pardon.

Structural Critique

The Crusades reveal what happens when Christianity merges with political power

The Crusades were possible because Constantine's merger of church and empire (4th century) had made the church an agent of state power. Anabaptist and radical Reformation thinkers argue this structural problem — not merely individual sin — requires a fundamental rethinking of the church-state relationship.
