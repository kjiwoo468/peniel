The Atonement: How Does Jesus Save?

IntroductionModerate tension

How does Jesus's death actually save?

Christians agree that Jesus's death and resurrection accomplish salvation. They disagree, often sharply, about the precise mechanism — the how. This is not a peripheral question: different understandings of atonement produce different understandings of God's character, human sin, and what the Gospel actually is. The New Testament itself offers multiple images, and the church has never formally defined one as the only valid account.

Original LanguageGreek study

The vocabulary of atonement in the New Testament

ἱλαστήριονhilastērionGreek

"Propitiation" or "expiation" or "mercy seat" — the most contested atonement word

This word appears in Romans 3:25 and Hebrews 9:5. In the Septuagint it translates the Hebrew word for the "mercy seat" — the lid of the ark of the covenant where the high priest sprinkled blood on the Day of Atonement. The precise meaning in Romans 3:25 is genuinely disputed: (1) propitiation — appeasing God's wrath (the more traditional Protestant reading); (2) expiation — cleansing sin without necessarily involving divine wrath (the reading favored by C.H. Dodd and many Catholic scholars); (3) "mercy seat" — a direct typological reference to the Day of Atonement ritual itself. The choice profoundly affects whether divine wrath is the central problem atonement addresses.

ἀντίλυτρονantilytronGreek

"A ransom paid instead / on behalf of" — Mark 10:45

"The Son of Man did not come to be served, but to serve, and to give his life as a ransom [lytron] for many." The "ransom" image suggests liberation from captivity — but the New Testament conspicuously never specifies to whom the ransom is paid. Early church theologians sometimes said "to the devil" (Origen, Gregory of Nyssa), a view later rejected; others said the logic is not transactional but metaphorical — liberation, not a literal payment to a creditor.

Historical Theories

How the church has understood atonement across history

🏆

Christus Victor (c. 100 AD – present)

The earliest dominant model: Christ's death and resurrection are the decisive victory over sin, death, and the devil — the cosmic powers that held humanity captive. Salvation is liberation, not a legal transaction. Dominant in patristic theology; still the primary model in Eastern Orthodox theology; recovered in the 20th century by Gustaf Aulén.

⚖️

Satisfaction Theory (Anselm of Canterbury, 1098)

Human sin dishonors God infinitely; only an infinite being (God become human) can render the satisfaction God's honor requires. Christ pays the debt humanity owes but cannot pay. A feudal logic: honor requires satisfaction; Christ provides it. Foundational for subsequent Western — both Catholic and Protestant — atonement theology.

⚖️

Penal Substitution (Reformation, 16th century)

God's justice requires punishment for sin; Christ absorbs that punishment in humanity's place. The problem is legal and retributive (not merely honorific as in Anselm): God must punish sin, and Christ is punished as our substitute. The dominant model in conservative Protestantism, especially evangelical. Calvin, Owen, Packer, Grudem.

❤️

Moral Influence / Exemplarist (Abelard, 12th century)

The cross demonstrates God's love so powerfully that it moves human hearts to repentance and moral transformation — the "problem" being not divine wrath requiring satisfaction but human hardness of heart requiring love's demonstration. Liberal Protestantism; some process theology; criticized by many evangelicals as insufficient to account for the objective necessity of Christ's death.

🌊

Scapegoat / Mimetic Theory (René Girard, 20th century)

Human society is built on sacrificial violence — channeling communal tension by targeting victims. Jesus is the innocent victim who exposes the scapegoat mechanism by refusing to return violence and rising from the dead, revealing that God is not the God of sacrifice and violence but the God who stands with victims. Influential in academic theology; Girard's background was literary theory, not theology, but his work has been widely adopted.

Scholarly Perspectives

What theologians say about atonement

Penal substitution is not one possible model among equals — it is the heart of the Gospel. Without it, the cross has no objective necessity; it is only a demonstration or an example. The New Testament's language of wrath, propitiation, and "for us" requires something stronger than an inspiring moral display.

J.I. PackerRegent College; author of In My Place Condemned He Stood (2007)

The New Testament offers a kaleidoscope of atonement images — sacrifice, ransom, victory, reconciliation, justification — and the church has never formally defined one as the exclusive account. Treating penal substitution as if it were formally defined dogma, equivalent in status to the Chalcedonian definition of Christ's person, overstates its doctrinal status.

N.T. WrightUniversity of St Andrews; author of The Day the Revolution Began (2016)

The Eastern Orthodox tradition has always understood atonement primarily as theosis — the participation of human nature in divine life — and Christus Victor, not as a legal transaction. It is a serious misreading of Christian history to treat forensic/penal models as the universal Christian tradition; they are a distinctly Western, largely post-Anselm development.

Alexander SchmemannSt. Vladimir's Orthodox Theological Seminary; leading 20th-century Orthodox theologian

Whatever model we use to explain atonement, we must hold together what the New Testament holds together: it is God who acts, not a third party being satisfied; it is for us as a whole — not just individual guilt but cosmic captivity — and it is accomplished through the resurrection as much as the cross. Easter is not an afterthought to Good Friday.

Jürgen MoltmannUniversity of Tübingen; author of The Crucified God (1972)

Christian Positions

The major atonement frameworks

Penal Substitution

Christ absorbs divine punishment as our legal substitute

God's justice requires punishment for sin. Christ, fully innocent, absorbs that punishment in our place. The just requirement of God's law is fulfilled; believers are declared righteous not by their works but by Christ's righteousness credited to them. Dominant in conservative evangelical and Reformed theology.

Christus Victor

Christ defeats the powers of sin, death, and evil

The primary problem is not divine wrath but cosmic captivity — to sin, death, and dark powers. Christ's death and resurrection are the decisive battle in which these powers are defeated and humanity liberated. Patristic and Eastern Orthodox tradition; recovered for modern theology by Gustaf Aulén.

Participatory / Union with Christ

Salvation comes through union with Christ's death and resurrection

Believers are saved not primarily by a legal transaction but by mystical participation in Christ — dying with him, rising with him. Paul's "in Christ" language (used over 200 times in his letters) is the primary framework. Eastern Orthodoxy; recovering influence in Protestant scholarship (Gorman, Campbell).

Multiple Models

No single theory exhausts what the New Testament says about the cross

The Bible uses sacrifice, ransom, reconciliation, victory, and moral demonstration as overlapping images, none of which the church has defined as exclusive. A robust atonement theology draws on all of them, recognizing that the reality of what Christ accomplished exceeds any single framework. N.T. Wright; many contemporary evangelical and Catholic theologians.
