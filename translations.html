Translation Controversies

← Scripture & Interpretation

Section 1

Introduction

Translation is always interpretation

Every translation requires thousands of judgment calls. No language maps perfectly onto another. This topic examines several translation controversies that carry real theological weight — and the KJV-Only movement that insists one 1611 English translation is itself inspired.

Controversy 1Fringe but notable

The KJV-Only movement

A minority but vocal movement within American fundamentalism holds that the King James Version (1611) is itself divinely preserved and superior to — or even more authoritative than — the underlying Greek and Hebrew texts.

What KJV-Only advocates argue

  • The KJV translators worked from the "Textus Receptus," a Greek text tradition they consider providentially preserved, as opposed to modern critical editions based on older manuscripts discovered since 1611 (including Codex Sinaiticus, found in 1844).
  • Modern translations, in their view, are based on a corrupted "Alexandrian" text type and were produced by scholars with theological liberal leanings.
  • Some hold "KJV inspiration" — that the English translation itself was providentially guided, not merely the original Hebrew and Greek.

The mainstream scholarly response

The vast majority of textual scholars — including conservative evangelical ones — reject KJV-Only claims. The manuscripts discovered after 1611 (including far older ones than anything available to the KJV translators) generally allow for a more accurate, not less accurate, reconstruction of the original text. The KJV itself was a translation, revised multiple times (1629, 1638, 1769), and contains demonstrable translation errors and now-archaic English that can mislead modern readers (e.g., "let" in 1611 English meant "hinder," the opposite of its modern meaning).

A telling example

The KJV translates 1 Thessalonians 4:15 as Christians who are alive "shall not prevent them which are asleep" — using "prevent" in its archaic sense of "precede." Modern readers naturally misread this as the opposite of its intended meaning.

Controversy 2Hebrew word study

"Virgin" or "young woman"? Isaiah 7:14

עַלְמָהalmahHebrew

Young woman of marriageable age (not specifically a virgin)

The Hebrew word in Isaiah 7:14 is almah, which generally refers to a young woman of childbearing age, with virginity implied culturally but not asserted by the word itself. Hebrew has a separate, more specific word for "virgin" — betulah — which Isaiah does not use here.

παρθένοςparthenosGreek

Virgin (specifically)

When the Septuagint (Greek translation of the Hebrew Bible, c. 3rd century BC) rendered Isaiah 7:14, the translators chose parthenos — a Greek word that does specifically mean "virgin." Matthew 1:23 then quotes this Greek version directly when applying the prophecy to Mary's virgin conception of Jesus.

Translation comparison

VersionIsaiah 7:14 rendering
KJV / NIV / ESV"virgin" — following the traditional Christological reading via the Septuagint and Matthew
NRSV"young woman" — reflecting the underlying Hebrew almah more literally; footnotes the traditional rendering
JPS (Jewish Publication Society)"young woman" — the standard Jewish translation, reflecting the original Hebrew sense and the original historical referent (likely a sign for King Ahaz's own time, not a far-future Messianic prophecy)

This is a genuinely significant translation choice: the Hebrew word alone does not require virginity, but the Greek word used by Matthew does. Most conservative Christian scholars argue Isaiah's prophecy operated on two levels — an immediate sign for Ahaz, and a deeper messianic fulfillment that the New Testament authors, guided by the Spirit, rightly discerned in the Septuagint's wording.

Controversy 3Greek word study

Why "servant" can be a misleading — or even dishonest — translation

δοῦλοςdoulosGreek

Slave (the primary and most direct meaning)

Doulos appears 124 times in the New Testament. Its primary, default meaning in Koine Greek is "slave" — a person who is owned. Many English translations, especially older ones, soften this to "servant" or "bondservant," which implies voluntary, paid employment rather than the ownership-based reality of ancient slavery.

Translation comparison — Romans 1:1, where Paul calls himself a doulos of Christ

VersionRendering
KJV"servant of Jesus Christ"
ESV / NIV"servant" (NIV) / "servant" (ESV, with footnote: "or slave; for the contextual rendering of the Greek word doulos...")
NRSV"slave of Jesus Christ"
CEB (Common English Bible)"slave of Christ Jesus"

Why this matters

Some scholars argue that softening doulos to "servant" throughout the New Testament has historically made it easier for readers — including American slaveholders — to avoid confronting how directly the New Testament addresses literal, owned slaves (see the Slavery topic in the History category). On the other hand, when Paul calls himself a "slave of Christ," some translators argue "servant" better captures the voluntary devotion Paul intends to convey to a modern audience, even if it understates the linguistic force of the original metaphor — Paul deliberately chose the most total, self-effacing image of submission available in his vocabulary.

Scholarly Perspectives

What translators and scholars say

Every translation is a series of compromises between fidelity to the original wording and clarity for the receiving language. There is no view from nowhere — translators always make theological choices, whether they admit it or not.

Eugene NidaLinguist; pioneer of "dynamic equivalence" Bible translation theory; American Bible Society

The persistent softening of doulos to "servant" across centuries of English translation is one of the more theologically consequential translation choices in the history of the English Bible — and not an innocent one.

Mitzi SmithWomanist biblical scholar; Columbia Theological Seminary

KJV-Onlyism is a modern invention, not a historic Christian doctrine. No translation, including the original KJV translators themselves, has ever claimed translational inerrancy for an English version. The KJV translators' own preface explicitly acknowledges their work could be improved.

D.A. CarsonTrinity Evangelical Divinity School; author of The King James Version Debate (1979)
