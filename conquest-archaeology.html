The Conquest of Canaan & Archaeology

← History & Justice

Section 1

IntroductionActive scholarly debate

What archaeology found at the conquest sites

The book of Joshua describes a swift, total military conquest of Canaan. A century of archaeological excavation at the named sites has produced results that significantly complicate this picture — and a major scholarly debate over what it means.

Case Study 1Archaeology

The walls that (probably) weren't there

🏛️
Excavated 1930–36 (Garstang); 1952–58 (Kenyon)

Kathleen Kenyon's excavation of Jericho (Tell es-Sultan)

British archaeologist Kathleen Kenyon's meticulous 1950s excavation — still considered the most rigorous study of the site — concluded that Jericho's Late Bronze Age walls had collapsed centuries earlier (associated with an Early/Middle Bronze Age destruction, c. 1550 BC) and that the site was largely unoccupied during the period most scholars associate with Joshua's conquest (either the traditional early date, c. 1400 BC, or the more commonly favored late date, c. 1230–1200 BC). Earlier excavator John Garstang had claimed to find collapsed walls dating to the conquest period, but Kenyon's more precise stratigraphic and pottery dating methods overturned this conclusion, a finding most subsequent archaeologists have accepted.

A dissenting voice

Archaeologist Bryant Wood has argued Kenyon's pottery dating was flawed and that Jericho's destruction can be redated to match the biblical conquest timeline. His position remains a minority view among professional archaeologists but is taken seriously by some conservative scholars and is not without all technical merit, keeping the question more open than popular presentations on either side often suggest.

Case Study 2Archaeology

Ai, Hazor, and the conquest pattern

🏚️

Ai (et-Tell)

The most commonly proposed site for biblical Ai shows no significant occupation during the relevant conquest period — the site appears to have been abandoned roughly a thousand years earlier and not resettled until centuries after Joshua's traditional dates. This is one of the more difficult archaeological puzzles in the conquest narrative, and some scholars now question whether et-Tell is even the correct site for biblical Ai.

🔥

Hazor

By contrast, excavations at Hazor (led by Yigael Yadin and later Amnon Ben-Tor) did find a major Late Bronze Age destruction layer with widespread burning, consistent with Joshua 11:10–13's specific claim that Hazor (unlike other conquered cities) was burned. This is one of the better archaeological correlations supporting at least a partial historical conquest tradition.

📊

The overall pattern

Of the cities Joshua claims were conquered, only a few (notably Hazor) show clear matching destruction layers; several (Jericho, Ai) show none for the relevant period; others have not been definitively located or excavated at all. This mixed picture has produced ongoing scholarly debate rather than a single settled conclusion.

Scholarly Perspectives

What archaeologists and theologians say

The archaeological evidence as a whole does not support a unified, rapid military conquest of Canaan as described in Joshua. It fits much better with a gradual, complex process of settlement — including military conflict in some places, peaceful infiltration in others, and significant continuity with existing Canaanite culture — closer to the more piecemeal picture in Judges 1 than the sweeping narrative of Joshua.

William DeverUniversity of Arizona; archaeologist

Joshua itself, read carefully, already signals a less total conquest than its summary statements suggest — note Joshua 13:1's admission that "very much land remains to be possessed" and the repeated notices of Canaanites still living among Israel (Joshua 15:63; 16:10; 17:12–13). The book's own internal tension between sweeping summary claims and granular admission of incomplete conquest deserves more attention than it typically receives.

K. Lawson Younger Jr.Trinity Evangelical Divinity School

Ancient Near Eastern royal/conquest narratives routinely used a literary convention of "summary totality" statements (claiming complete, total victory) immediately followed by narrower, more specific accounts that implicitly qualify the totalizing claim. Reading Joshua within this genre convention, rather than as modern military historiography, resolves much (not all) of the apparent tension between sweeping claims and archaeological evidence.

K.A. KitchenUniversity of Liverpool; Egyptologist

Christian Positions

How Christians interpret the evidence

Defended Historicity

The conquest happened largely as described; archaeology is incomplete or misdated

Archaeological gaps reflect the limits and uncertainties of dating methods, incomplete excavation, and site identification — not disproof. Hazor's matching destruction layer demonstrates the narrative's basic historical reliability where evidence is available.

Gradual Settlement Model

A complex, gradual process lies behind the unified conquest narrative

Israel emerged in Canaan through a mix of limited military conflict, peaceful settlement, and internal Canaanite social transformation (some scholars propose a "peasant revolt" model) — later compiled into a more unified, theologically shaped conquest narrative emphasizing God's faithfulness to the covenant promise of the land.

Theological Priority

The text's spiritual message stands regardless of the precise historical reconstruction

Joshua's central theological claims — God's faithfulness, the danger of idolatry, the gift of the land — remain authoritative and meaningful whether or not every archaeological detail of the conquest can be confirmed by excavation.

See: Dever, Who Were the Early Israelites? (2003); Kenyon, Digging Up Jericho (1957); Younger, Ancient Conquest Accounts (1990); Hess, Joshua: An Introduction and Commentary (1996).
