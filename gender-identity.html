IntroductionHigh tension

What does the Bible say about gender and the body?

Questions about transgender and nonbinary identities are among the most contested the church faces today. The Bible addresses gender with more nuance than either side of popular debate typically acknowledges. The key texts are real, but so is their complexity — and the pastoral stakes are extraordinarily high, with LGBTQ+ youth experiencing alarmingly high rates of depression and suicidality in unsupportive environments.

Key TextsHebrew & Greek study

Creation, eunuchs, and the body

"So God created mankind in his own image, in the image of God he created them; male and female he created them."Genesis 1:27
זָכָר וּנְקֵבָהzakar u-nekeivahHebrew

"Male and female" — a merism describing the full range, not a closed binary

Scholars note that Hebrew uses merisms — naming two extremes to include everything between them — throughout Genesis 1: "heaven and earth" includes everything between, not only sky and ground; "evening and morning" includes the full day. Some theologians argue "male and female" functions as a similar merism: the creation of the full spectrum of human sexual embodiment, not a claim that every human falls precisely into one of only two categories. This reading remains disputed — many conservative scholars reject the merism interpretation in this context — but it is exegetically serious, not merely motivated.

"For there are eunuchs who were born that way, and there are eunuchs who have been made eunuchs by others — and there are those who choose to live like eunuchs for the sake of the kingdom of heaven."Matthew 19:12

The significance of Matthew 19:12

Jesus acknowledges three categories of eunuchs: those who were "born that way" (eunouchoi hoi... ek koilias — literally "from the womb"), those castrated by others, and those who chose celibacy. "Born eunuchs" in the ancient world covered a range of people whose bodies or sexuality didn't fit standard categories — potentially including intersex individuals and those with gender variance. Significantly, Jesus expresses no condemnation but treats these as simply categories of human reality. Isaiah 56:3–5 explicitly promises eunuchs an honored place in God's house, "better than sons and daughters."

Scientific Context

What biology reveals about sex and gender

Intersex conditions complicate a simple binary

  • Approximately 1.7% of the population is born with intersex conditions — variations in sex characteristics including chromosomes, gonads, sex hormones, or genitals that do not fit typical binary definitions of male or female. This is not a political claim but a medical fact, and it complicates "male and female only, always clearly distinguishable" as a description of biological reality.
  • The brain and body can develop along partially different sex-differentiation pathways in utero. Neurobiological research has found differences in brain structure and activation patterns associated with gender dysphoria, though the research is contested and not definitively settled.
  • "Sex" (biological) and "gender" (social/psychological sense of self) have been distinguished analytically in medical and psychological contexts since the 1950s. The theological question is partly about what weight should be given to each.

How Christians engage this scientific complexity varies: some treat intersex conditions as evidence that the male-female binary is itself a creation norm that sin has disrupted; others treat it as evidence that human embodiment is more complex than the binary framework assumes.

Scholarly Perspectives

What theologians and ethicists say

The male-female distinction in Genesis 1 is grounded in the creation order, not merely in culture, and reflects God's design for human flourishing and the family. Affirming a gender identity at odds with biological sex treats the body as raw material for self-expression rather than as a gift to be received. This does not mean harshness toward those who suffer — it requires extraordinary pastoral compassion — but it shapes what faithfulness looks like.

Andrew WalkerSouthern Baptist Theological Seminary; author of God and the Transgender Debate (2017)

Transgender people exist, experience profound distress, and are made in the image of God. The pastoral question is not primarily "what does the Bible say about gender dysphoria as a category?" — it largely doesn't address it — but "how does the church reflect Christ's welcome to people whose embodied experience doesn't fit the dominant cultural script?"

Megan DeFranzaAuthor of Sex Difference in Christian Theology: Male, Female, and Intersex in the Image of God (2015)

The church's greatest need on this question is not a quicker, more confident answer, but greater willingness to sit with people in their actual experience — listening before pronouncing, and recognizing that pastoral faithfulness and dogmatic clarity are not always the same thing.

Wesley HillTrinity School for Ministry; author of Washed and Waiting (2010); writing on sexuality and pastoral care

Christian Positions

The major positions

Binary Creational View

God created two complementary sexes; gender aligns with biological sex

Genesis 1:27's "male and female" establishes the normative framework for human embodiment. Gender dysphoria is a real condition requiring pastoral compassion, but the appropriate response is to support the person in aligning their gender identity with biological sex — not to affirm a cross-sex identity. Medical transition conflicts with the integrity of the body as God's gift. Most traditional Catholic, Orthodox, and conservative evangelical positions.

Pastoral Accommodation View

Relief from severe dysphoria may justify some gender transition for wellbeing

A mediating position: while affirming the male-female binary as creational, recognizes that severe, persistent gender dysphoria is a real condition causing acute suffering, and that supporting some individuals in social transition may be consistent with care for the whole person, even if ideal. The medical and pastoral judgment is complex and not reducible to a single rule. Held by some evangelical ethicists and pastors.

Affirming View

Transgender identities reflect genuine human diversity and may be embraced

Genesis's "male and female" is a merism or cultural description, not an exhaustive biological taxonomy. Matthew 19:12's "born eunuchs" and intersex biological reality suggest human sexual embodiment is more varied than a strict binary. A faithful response affirms the transgender person's identity and supports gender-affirming care as consistent with human dignity and flourishing. Episcopal Church, ELCA, UCC, and other affirming traditions.

Pastoral Priority

Welcome and relationship first; doctrinal resolution secondary

Many congregations and pastors believe the most pressing need is not a theological position statement but genuine relationship and welcome with trans people. Doctrinal clarity, while important, can be held in tension with immediate pastoral care and accompaniment.
