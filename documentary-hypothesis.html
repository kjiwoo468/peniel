The Documentary Hypothesis

Section 1

Introduction

Did Moses write the Pentateuch?

Jewish and Christian tradition has long attributed the first five books of the Bible (the Torah / Pentateuch) to Moses. Since the 19th century, however, mainstream biblical scholarship has argued these books were compiled from multiple older sources, woven together over centuries. This is one of the most foundational — and most contested — claims of modern biblical scholarship.

The TheoryScholarly consensus, contested by some

The JEDP / Documentary Hypothesis

Formalized by German scholar Julius Wellhausen in 1878 (building on earlier work by Jean Astruc and others), the theory identifies four main source documents woven into the Pentateuch:

J

The Yahwist (J)

Uses the divine name YHWH ("Yahweh") from the beginning; vivid, anthropomorphic depictions of God; traditionally dated earliest (10th–9th century BC), associated with the southern kingdom of Judah.

E

The Elohist (E)

Uses "Elohim" for God until the revelation of the divine name to Moses (Exodus 3); associated with the northern kingdom of Israel, dated slightly later than J.

D

The Deuteronomist (D)

Comprises most of Deuteronomy; distinct theological and legal style; many scholars link it to the law book "discovered" during Josiah's temple renovation (2 Kings 22, c. 622 BC).

P

The Priestly source (P)

Detailed genealogies, ritual law, and the Genesis 1 creation account; concerned with priesthood, sacrifice, and ritual purity; often dated to the exilic or post-exilic period (6th–5th century BC).

The textual evidence cited

  • Doublets: Genesis contains two creation accounts (Gen 1:1–2:3 and 2:4–25) with different styles, divine names, and even a different order of creation. Two flood narratives appear interwoven in Genesis 6–9, with apparent inconsistencies in the number of animals taken aboard (compare Gen 6:19–20 with 7:2–3).
  • Different divine names: Genesis sections consistently use either "Elohim" or "YHWH" for God, rarely mixing them within a single narrative strand — a pattern that struck early source critics as significant.
  • Anachronisms: Genesis 36:31 references "kings who reigned in Edom before any Israelite king reigned" — language that seems to presuppose the existence of Israelite kings, centuries after Moses's traditional lifetime.
  • Stylistic and theological variation: Legal codes in Exodus, Leviticus, and Deuteronomy show different emphases and even differing details on the same laws (e.g., regulations on Hebrew slaves differ between Exodus 21 and Deuteronomy 15).
Modern DevelopmentsArchaeology

How the field has evolved since Wellhausen

The classical four-source JEDP theory, while still influential, is no longer held in its original 1878 form by most scholars. Decades of further study have complicated the picture.

📜
20th century

Comparative Ancient Near Eastern literature

Discovery of texts like the Code of Hammurabi (c. 1750 BC) and the Epic of Gilgamesh showed that ancient Near Eastern legal and narrative literature was often compiled, redacted, and revised over long periods — providing a cultural context that makes a similarly complex compositional history for the Pentateuch more plausible, not less.

🔍
1970s–present

The fragmentation of consensus

Many scholars (e.g., Rolf Rendtorff, Erhard Blum) have abandoned the idea of four continuous, complete source documents in favor of more complex models involving smaller blocks of tradition combined and edited over time. Others (the "Copenhagen school," including Niels Peter Lemche) push composition dates much later, into the Persian or even Hellenistic period.

🏺
Ongoing

Conservative scholarly responses

Scholars like Umberto Cassuto, Kenneth Kitchen, and Gordon Wenham have argued that many of the "doublets" and stylistic variations can be explained as intentional ancient literary techniques (repetition for emphasis, varying divine names for theological reasons depending on context) rather than evidence of separate sources — and that the genuine antiquity of much Pentateuchal legal and narrative material is supported by parallels to genuinely early second-millennium BC Near Eastern customs.

Scholarly Perspectives

What scholars across the spectrum say

The Documentary Hypothesis in its classical form is dead, but source-critical analysis of the Pentateuch's composite nature is not. What has changed is our confidence in reconstructing precise, continuous documents — not our recognition that the text has a complex compositional history.

Joel BadenYale Divinity School; author of The Composition of the Pentateuch (2012)

Mosaic authorship, in the sense of Moses as the primary source and shaping authority behind the Pentateuch's core legal and narrative material, remains defensible — even while allowing for later editorial updating (place names, the account of Moses's own death in Deuteronomy 34) that any ancient or modern reader would expect.

Kenneth KitchenUniversity of Liverpool; Egyptologist; author of On the Reliability of the Old Testament (2003)

The two creation accounts in Genesis 1–2 are not best read as competing, contradictory sources to be harmonized or dissected, but as complementary literary panels serving different theological purposes — a literary observation independent of source-critical questions about authorship.

John WaltonWheaton College; author of The Lost World of Genesis One (2009)

Christian Positions

How Christians respond to the hypothesis

Traditional Mosaic Authorship

Moses wrote the Pentateuch substantially as we have it

Jesus and New Testament authors refer to "the Law of Moses" and attribute specific texts to him (e.g., Mark 12:26, John 5:46). The "doublets" reflect intentional ancient literary structure (repetition, parallelism), not separate sources. Minor post-Mosaic updates (like Genesis 36:31, or the account of Moses's death) are acknowledged as later scribal additions, but do not undermine essential Mosaic authorship of the core text.

Critical Acceptance

The Pentateuch is a composite work; this does not threaten its inspiration

God can inspire a text compiled by multiple human authors and editors over centuries just as fully as a text written by one person. The Documentary Hypothesis (in its modified modern forms) describes the human process; divine inspiration operates through and not despite that process. This is broadly the view of many mainline Protestant, Catholic, and some evangelical Old Testament scholars today.

Agnostic on Composition

The question of sources is secondary to the text's final canonical form

"Canonical criticism" (associated with Brevard Childs) argues that whatever the compositional history, the church received and should interpret the Pentateuch in its final, unified canonical shape — making source-critical reconstruction theologically interesting but not decisive for how Scripture should be read and applied today.
