IntroductionHigh tension

God commands genocide — how do we read this?

In Numbers, Deuteronomy, and Joshua, God commands Israel to utterly destroy entire nations — killing men, women, children, and animals. These are detailed military orders attributed directly to God. This is perhaps the single hardest question in all of biblical theology.

Original LanguageHebrew study

Herem — the ban of total destruction

חֵרֶםheremHebrew

"The ban" — total devotion to destruction, irrevocably set apart for God

This technical term describes something or someone permanently consecrated to God through total destruction — nothing could be kept as plunder; everything was either destroyed or given to the sanctuary (as in Joshua 6:17–19 with Jericho's silver and gold). The concept has parallels in other ancient Near Eastern texts: the 9th-century BC Moabite Stone (Mesha Stele) describes King Mesha applying the identical concept (using a cognate Semitic root) to his own conquest of an Israelite town — suggesting herem warfare was a known regional practice, not a uniquely Israelite innovation.

ArchaeologyPhysical evidence

What the archaeological record shows

🏺
Excavated 1952–1958

Jericho

British archaeologist Kathleen Kenyon's excavations found that Jericho's massive Bronze Age walls had already collapsed and the city lay largely unoccupied by the time scholars typically date the conquest (c. 1400–1200 BC), based on pottery analysis. This has fueled significant scholarly debate over the historicity of Joshua 6, with dating methodologies themselves disputed (see the Conquest of Canaan topic for fuller discussion).

📜
9th century BC

The Mesha Stele (Moabite Stone)

Discovered in 1868, this inscription by King Mesha of Moab boasts of devoting an Israelite town to his god Chemosh through total destruction — using the same root concept as Hebrew herem. This confirms herem-style warfare rhetoric was a shared regional religious-military convention in the ancient Near East, not a uniquely biblical horror.

📋
Various, 2nd–1st millennium BC

Comparative ANE conquest accounts

Egyptian, Assyrian, and Hittite royal inscriptions routinely employ hyperbolic, totalizing destruction language ("I left not one alive," "I burned their cities and sowed them with salt") for conquests that other evidence shows were considerably less total in practice — a literary convention of royal triumph rhetoric scholars call the genre of "conquest accounts."

Scholarly Perspectives

What modern scholars say

"Utterly destroy" was conventional ancient Near Eastern military hyperbole — boastful, formulaic language that did not necessarily mean every single person was killed. The biblical text itself preserves evidence against total destruction: Rahab and her family survive (Joshua 6:25), the Gibeonites survive through deception (Joshua 9), and Canaanites are explicitly still present generations later (Judges 1:21, 27–33).

K. Lawson Younger Jr.Trinity Evangelical Divinity School; author of Ancient Conquest Accounts (1990)

God is the sovereign author of life and has the right to execute judgment on persistent, severe evil. Genesis 15:16 explicitly frames the conquest as delayed judgment — "the sin of the Amorites has not yet reached its full measure" — not arbitrary violence against an innocent people.

Paul CopanPalm Beach Atlantic University; author of Is God a Moral Monster? (2011)

Some Old Testament texts reflect Israel's own developing, imperfect understanding of God's character — an understanding that the fuller revelation in Jesus Christ, who explicitly rejects calling down violent judgment (Luke 9:54–55), corrects and surpasses. We should read the Old Testament's violence through, not apart from, the lens of Christ.

Greg BoydWoodland Hills Church; author of The Crucifixion of the Warrior God (2017)

I do not think we should pretend these texts are easy. C.S. Lewis himself, writing about the imprecatory Psalms, acknowledged a moral development across Scripture and was willing to say some Old Testament attitudes reflect a less mature stage of revelation than what Christ later brings. Intellectual honesty sometimes means sitting with real difficulty rather than manufacturing tidy solutions.

Eric SeibertMessiah University; author of The Violence of Scripture (2012)

Christian Positions

Five theological responses

Divine Sovereignty & Judgment

God has the right to take human life; the Canaanites were judged

God is the author of all life with sovereign right to end it. The Canaanites had "filled up the measure of their sin." Israel was an instrument of divine judgment, as Babylon would later be against Israel itself. Most Reformed and traditional evangelical scholarship.

Hyperbolic War Rhetoric

"Total destruction" was ancient war-speak, not literal

Comparative ANE texts show this language was conventional military hyperbole. Internal biblical evidence (Rahab, the Gibeonites, surviving Canaanites in Judges) supports a more limited historical reality than the rhetoric suggests.

Progressive Revelation

Israel's understanding of God was still developing

The OT records Israel's growing — and sometimes mistaken — understanding of God. Some violent commands may reflect Israel's own cultural assumptions projected onto God. The fuller revelation of God's character comes in Christ.

Honest Moral Struggle

This is genuinely troubling; intellectual honesty requires saying so

Rather than manufacturing a full solution, some theologians sit with the tension, trusting the God revealed in Christ is not the God who desires infant slaughter, while acknowledging they cannot fully explain the texts.

Non-Literal / Liturgical Reading

These texts function theologically, not as historical military reports

Some scholars read the conquest narratives as later theological compositions (written centuries after any historical events they describe) meant to articulate Israel's covenant exclusivity and the dangers of idolatry — not as straightforward historical reportage of literal genocide.

See: Copan, Is God a Moral Monster? (2011); Seibert, The Violence of Scripture (2012); Boyd, The Crucifixion of the Warrior God (2017); Younger, Ancient Conquest Accounts (1990).
