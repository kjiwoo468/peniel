Women in Church Leadership

← Ethics & Human Sexuality

Section 1

IntroductionHigh tension

Can women lead and preach in the church?

Two passages in Paul's letters appear to restrict women from teaching or holding authority over men. Yet other passages — and Jesus's own practice — point a different direction. This topic examines the disputed Greek vocabulary and the full range of positions.

Key Texts

The texts that appear to restrict women's leadership

"I do not permit a woman to teach or to assume authority over a man; she must be quiet."1 Timothy 2:12
"Women should remain silent in the churches. They are not allowed to speak, but must be in submission, as the law says."1 Corinthians 14:34

The Greek word at the center of the debate

αὐθεντεῖνauthenteinGreek

"To have/exercise authority" — but possibly with a more specific, negative connotation

This word appears only once in the entire New Testament (1 Timothy 2:12) — a "hapax legomenon," meaning we cannot compare Paul's usage elsewhere in his letters to clarify intent. Outside the New Testament, the word's range in surviving Greek literature is unusually broad and disputed: some classical and Koine sources use it for legitimate authority; others use it for domineering, violent, or even murderous control — its root is related to authentēs, which in some contexts meant "murderer" or "perpetrator." Linguist Cynthia Westfall and others argue the rarity of the word, combined with its possible negative connotations elsewhere, suggests Paul may be prohibiting a domineering, improper exercise of power — not ordinary teaching authority as such.

Translation comparison — 1 Timothy 2:12

VersionRendering
KJV"usurp authority over the man" — notably, "usurp" implies illegitimate seizure, not just any exercise of authority
ESV (2001)"exercise authority over a man" — neutral rendering, widely used by complementarians
NIV (2011)"assume authority over a man"
NRSV"have authority over a man"
CEB"have authority over a man"

A notable translation history

The KJV's choice of "usurp" (implying wrongful seizure) was actually closer to some ancient usage patterns than many modern "neutral authority" translations — illustrating how translation choices have shifted the apparent scope of Paul's prohibition over centuries, in directions that don't track a simple "older = more conservative" pattern.

Counter-Evidence

Women in biblical leadership

Women who held recognized authority in Scripture

  • Deborah (Judges 4–5) — a judge over all Israel, military leader, and prophet, exercising the highest civil and religious authority recognized in her era.
  • Huldah (2 Kings 22:14–20) — a prophet consulted directly by King Josiah's officials to authenticate the newly discovered Book of the Law, ahead of any male prophet of the time including Jeremiah.
  • Philip's four daughters (Acts 21:9) — described simply as prophets, exercising a recognized teaching/revelatory gift in the early church.
  • Priscilla (Acts 18:26) — along with her husband Aquila, "explained to him [Apollos] the way of God more accurately" — a teaching role over a man, notably listed first ahead of her husband in several manuscripts.
  • The women at the resurrection — Jesus appeared first to women and commissioned them to tell the male disciples, effectively making them the first proclaimers ("apostles" in the literal sense of "sent ones") of the resurrection.

Junia — "outstanding among the apostles"

ἸουνίανIounianGreek

Junia — a woman's name

Romans 16:7 reads: "Greet Andronicus and Junia... they are outstanding among the apostles." For centuries, many translators assumed this must be a man's name (Junias), since a female apostle seemed implausible to them. Modern textual and onomastic scholarship has shown decisively that "Junias" as a male name is unattested anywhere in ancient Greek literature, while "Junia" is a well-documented Roman female name. Early church fathers, including John Chrysostom (4th century), read the name as female without controversy: "How great the wisdom of this woman that she should be counted worthy of the appellation of apostle!" Most modern translations (NIV 2011, ESV, NRSV) now render the name as the feminine "Junia."

Scholarly Perspectives

What modern scholars say

The case for restricting authentein to a narrow, negative sense ("to domineer") is linguistically possible but not proven by the limited evidence we have. We simply don't have enough surviving uses of this rare word to be fully confident either way — intellectual honesty requires admitting the uncertainty.

Andreas KöstenbergerSoutheastern Baptist Theological Seminary; co-editor of Women in the Church (complementarian)

1 Timothy was written into a specific situation in Ephesus, likely involving false teaching spread by some women in the congregation (compare 1 Timothy 5:13 — "going about from house to house... saying things they ought not to"). Paul's instruction addresses that local crisis, not a timeless universal principle.

Gordon FeeRegent College; New Testament scholar; egalitarian

The full sweep of biblical narrative — Deborah, Huldah, Junia, the women at the empty tomb — shows God consistently using women in roles of spiritual authority, even within a patriarchal culture. That pattern carries real interpretive weight alongside Paul's restrictive statements.

N.T. WrightFormer Bishop of Durham; New Testament scholar

Christian Positions

The major positions

Complementarianism

Equal in worth; different in role

Men and women bear equal dignity, but the office of elder/pastor is reserved for qualified men, rooted in creation order, not cultural accommodation. SBC, PCA, many evangelical churches. Piper, Grudem, CBMW.

Egalitarianism

The restrictive passages are culturally conditioned

Paul's restrictions address a specific first-century crisis. The wider biblical witness — Deborah, Huldah, Junia, Galatians 3:28 — points toward full inclusion in ministry. Assemblies of God, many Anglican/Methodist/Baptist churches. CBE, N.T. Wright, Gordon Fee.

Catholic / Orthodox View

Male priesthood reflects theological necessity

The priest acts in persona Christi; Christ was incarnate as male and chose only male Apostles. Not a matter of capability but of theological symbolism. Women hold many essential non-ordained ministries.

Soft Complementarianism

Women may serve broadly; senior pastor is male

A mediating position permitting women to preach and lead in most contexts while reserving the senior/lead pastoral office for men.
