IntroductionHigh tension

Can Christians ever kill in war?

Jesus commanded "love your enemies" and "blessed are the peacemakers." Paul commanded "do not repay evil with evil." Yet Romans 13 affirms the state's sword, and David — the warrior king — is called "a man after God's own heart." From the earliest centuries, Christians have disagreed sharply on whether participation in warfare is ever permissible, and under what conditions.

The TraditionHistorical record

Augustine's framework and its seven criteria

📚
c. 426 AD

Augustine of Hippo, The City of God

Facing the fall of Rome and questions about Christian soldiers, Augustine articulated the first systematic Christian just war theory. War may be permissible only when fought by legitimate authority, for a just cause, with right intention (restoration of peace, not conquest or glory). Later tradition, codified through Thomas Aquinas and subsequent Catholic moral theology, expanded these into seven criteria: legitimate authority, just cause, right intention, last resort, probability of success, proportionality of means, and discrimination (non-combatant immunity).

"Blessed are the peacemakers, for they will be called children of God."Matthew 5:9
"Do not suppose that I have come to bring peace to the earth. I did not come to bring peace, but a sword."Matthew 10:34

The "sword" in Matthew 10:34

This verse — Jesus's most militaristic-sounding statement — is almost universally read by scholars, including those who support just war theory, as metaphorical: the "sword" of division that Jesus's claims bring within families, not a call to armed conflict. The immediate context (vv. 35–36) confirms this: the division is between family members who accept or reject Jesus, not between nations at war.

Christian Pacifism

The Anabaptist and nonviolent resistance tradition

"You have heard that it was said, 'Love your neighbor and hate your enemy.' But I tell you, love your enemies and pray for those who persecute you."Matthew 5:43–44

The pre-Constantinian church and the pacifist argument

  • Before the conversion of Emperor Constantine (312 AD) made Christianity the empire's favored religion, the overwhelming majority of surviving Christian literature opposes Christian participation in warfare. Tertullian, Origen, and Cyprian all explicitly argue against it. This is the strongest historical argument for pacifism: the church before it was politically powerful was largely pacifist.
  • The Anabaptist Reformation (1525 onward) recovered this pre-Constantinian ethic, arguing that the Constantinian merging of church and empire was the decisive corruption. Christians serve a different kingdom, under different rules — including enemy love — that cannot be suspended for national interest.
  • John Howard Yoder's influential argument: Jesus's cross was not a tragic detour but the embodiment of God's political strategy. Disciples are called to the same "politics of the cross" — absorbing rather than retaliating against evil.
Scholarly Perspectives

What theologians say

The just war tradition does not baptize war or endorse violence as good. It provides criteria under which the state's tragic, last-resort use of force may be morally permitted — a very different thing from enthusiasm for war or belief that God is on "our side."

Oliver O'DonovanUniversity of Edinburgh; author of The Just War Revisited (2003)

The church did not begin to accommodate participation in war for principled theological reasons — it did so because the church had become the empire's religious arm after Constantine. What was once a theologian's argument became an ideology of conquest. The pacifist tradition is not naive; it is the original Christian position, recovered by communities serious about following Jesus rather than governing Rome.

John Howard YoderUniversity of Notre Dame; Mennonite theologian; author of The Politics of Jesus (1972)

Christian pacifism correctly identifies the cross as the paradigm for Christian ethics but risks confusing what is demanded of individuals with what is permitted for states. Paul's recognition of the state's coercive authority (Romans 13) introduces a distinction between personal ethics and political responsibility that pacifism collapses too quickly.

Reinhold NiebuhrUnion Theological Seminary; author of Moral Man and Immoral Society (1932)

The deep problem with just war theory is not the criteria themselves but their application: every nation in every war has believed its cause was just, its authorities legitimate, and its enemy the aggressor. The tradition's criteria are not self-applying — they require honest, critical judgment that nations in wartime rarely exercise about themselves.

Stanley HauerwasDuke Divinity School; Christian pacifist; author of The Peaceable Kingdom (1983)

Christian Positions

The four major positions

Just War Theory

War is sometimes morally permissible under strict conditions

The state's God-given authority includes legitimate use of coercive force. Augustine's criteria — just cause, legitimate authority, right intention, proportionality, last resort — provide a framework for moral judgment about specific wars. Roman Catholicism, mainline Protestantism, most evangelical traditions.

Christian Pacifism

Christians may never participate in war or killing

Jesus's command to love enemies and the cross as God's paradigmatic response to violence requires absolute nonparticipation in killing. Christians witness to a different kingdom by refusing the sword, even at personal cost. Mennonites, Church of the Brethren, Quakers, many Anabaptist communities.

Nonviolent Resistance

Force may be used to protect others but killing is prohibited

Christians may take protective action — including physical intervention — to defend the vulnerable, but deliberately killing remains prohibited. A mediating position between just war and absolute pacifism, emphasizing the active character of nonviolence (not passive submission). Associated with some strands of liberation theology and Christian social activism.

Holy War / Crusade

God can directly command war; Christians may fight for God's cause

God commanded Israel's wars in the OT; Christian nations can similarly be called to fight for God's purposes. This position — historically influential in the Crusades, the Reconquista, and some Christian nationalist movements — is rejected by most contemporary Christian ethicists as a confusion of national interest with divine mandate. Largely a historical position, though traces persist in some political theologies.
