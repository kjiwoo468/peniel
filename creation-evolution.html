IntroductionHigh tension

Are Genesis and evolution compatible?

Evolution is the organizing framework of modern biology. Genesis 1–2 describes creation in six days, with humanity formed specially. For some Christians these are irreconcilably opposed; for others they answer different questions entirely. This is one of the most consequential interpretive disagreements in contemporary Christianity.

Original LanguageHebrew study

Yom — does "day" mean a 24-hour day?

יוֹםyomHebrew

"Day" — can mean a 24-hour period, daylight hours, or a longer indefinite era, depending on context

Yom appears over 2,300 times in the Hebrew Bible with a demonstrably wide semantic range. It can mean: a literal 24-hour day (the most common usage), the daylight portion only (as in Genesis 1:5's "God called the light 'day'"), or a longer, indefinite period (as in Genesis 2:4, "in the day [yom] that the LORD God made the earth and the heavens" — referring to the entire creation process spanning Genesis 1's six "days," and the common phrase "the day of the Lord," referring to an extended era of judgment, not 24 hours). Young Earth advocates note that when yom is modified by a number (as in "the first day," "the second day" — the pattern throughout Genesis 1), it overwhelmingly means a literal day elsewhere in Hebrew narrative. Old Earth advocates note that the sun and moon — the very mechanism for measuring a literal solar day — are not created until "day four" (Genesis 1:14–19), complicating a strictly literal 24-hour reading of days one through three.

Literary Structure

What the genre of Genesis 1 suggests

Literary features scholars note

  • Genesis 1 has a highly patterned, repetitive structure: a refrain ("and God saw that it was good"), a numbered formula ("and there was evening, and there was morning — the Nth day"), and a deliberate parallel structure where days 1–3 (forming realms: light/dark, sky/sea, land) match days 4–6 (filling those realms: sun/moon/stars, birds/fish, land animals/humans) — a literary symmetry many scholars argue signals intentional, crafted theological poetry rather than a straightforward chronological news report.
  • Genesis 1 and 2 present two creation accounts with different emphases, vocabulary, and even ordering (in Genesis 2, the man is formed before plants and animals, reversing Genesis 1's sequence) — read by many scholars as two complementary theological "panels" rather than a single continuous chronological narrative (see also the Documentary Hypothesis topic).
Scholarly Perspectives

What scientists and theologians say

As a geneticist who has studied the human genome in great depth, I find the evidence for common descent overwhelming and beautiful — and I find no conflict whatsoever with a robust, orthodox Christian faith. Evolution is how God created.

Francis CollinsFormer director, National Institutes of Health; led the Human Genome Project; author of The Language of God (2006); founder of BioLogos

Genesis 1 is best read as describing the cosmos in terms of its functions being assigned, within an ancient Near Eastern "cosmic temple" framework — not as a scientific account of material origins. Asking whether it agrees with modern science is asking the text a question it was never written to answer.

John WaltonWheaton College; author of The Lost World of Genesis One (2009)

A straightforward, plain reading of Genesis — the reading the text's original audience would most naturally have understood — supports six literal days of creation. Departing from that plain reading should require very strong justification, and I don't believe extrapolated scientific consensus, which has been wrong before, provides it.

Ken HamAnswers in Genesis; young earth creationist

The fossil and genetic evidence for common descent is not seriously disputed within mainstream biology. Christians do their witness no favors by treating this as an open scientific question — the credible theological conversation is about how to integrate this established science with faith, not whether to accept it.

Dennis VenemaTrinity Western University; biologist; senior fellow at BioLogos

Christian Positions

The four major positions

Young Earth Creationism

Six literal 24-hour days, ~6,000–10,000 years ago

Yom throughout Genesis 1 means a standard day. Where scientific data appear to indicate great age, the data are misinterpreted or God created with apparent age. Answers in Genesis, Institute for Creation Research; held by a significant minority of American evangelicals.

Old Earth Creationism

Ancient earth; various readings of Genesis allow long ages; humans specially created

Day-Age, Gap Theory, and Framework Hypothesis readings all allow an ancient universe. Humans, however, were specially and miraculously created — not through an evolutionary process. Hugh Ross / Reasons to Believe.

Evolutionary Creationism

God created all things through the process of evolution

Genesis 1–2 is theological literature answering who and why, not how and when. Evolution is God's method of creation; humans remain genuinely special — image-bearers with moral and spiritual capacity — while sharing common biological descent. BioLogos; Francis Collins; most scientists who are Christians.

Intelligent Design

The complexity of life points to a designing intelligence

A scientific (not strictly biblical-interpretive) argument that certain biological features are best explained by intelligent cause rather than undirected process. Held by both young-earth and old-earth Christians. Discovery Institute; Michael Behe; William Dembski.

See: Collins, The Language of God (2006); Ross, A Matter of Days; Morris, The Genesis Record; Walton, The Lost World of Genesis One (2009).
