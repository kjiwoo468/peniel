Abortion: What the Bible Actually Says

IntroductionHigh tension

What does the Bible actually say about abortion?

Perhaps surprisingly, the Bible never directly and explicitly addresses induced abortion as we understand it today. Both sides of the contemporary debate draw on texts that address related but distinct questions — the value of unborn life, accidental miscarriage, and personhood. This topic examines those texts honestly, in their original Hebrew.

The Central Disputed TextHebrew study

The most contested passage

"If people are fighting and hit a pregnant woman and she gives birth prematurely but there is no serious injury, the offender must be fined... But if there is serious injury, you are to take life for life..."Exodus 21:22–23
יָצְאוּ יְלָדֶיהָyatze'u yeladehaHebrew

Literally: "her children/offspring come out"

This is the crux of the entire passage. Does the Hebrew describe (a) a miscarriage — the fetus dies, with only a financial fine, implying the fetus has lesser legal status than the mother — or (b) a premature but live birth, with "serious injury" (Hebrew ason) referring to harm to either mother or child, implying equal protection? Hebrew scholars are genuinely divided. The word for "children/offspring" (yeladeha) is the ordinary word for living children elsewhere in the Old Testament, which some argue favors the "premature live birth" reading.

Translation comparison

VersionRenderingImplication
KJV (1611)"so that her fruit depart from her" — ambiguous, doesn't specify live or deadNeutral
RSV (1952)"so that there is a miscarriage" — explicitly interprets as fetal deathLower view of fetal status (mere property damage)
NIV (1984)"and she gives birth prematurely" — explicitly interprets as live premature birthHigher view of fetal status (equal protection)
NIV (2011 revision)"so that she gives birth prematurely" — retained the 1984 reading after reviewSame as above
ESV"so that her children come out" — more literal, leaves ambiguity intactNeutral

Why this single translation choice matters so much

The 1952 RSV's "miscarriage" reading became a significant pro-choice proof text for decades (implying Scripture treats fetal loss as a lesser, finable offense compared to harming the mother). The shift to "premature birth" in the NIV and other modern translations significantly altered the verse's apparent implications for fetal personhood — a vivid, high-stakes example of how a translator's Hebrew judgment call can shape a major ethical debate.

Other Key Texts

What else is cited on each side

"For you created my inmost being; you knit me together in my mother's womb."Psalm 139:13
"Before I formed you in the womb I knew you, before you were born I set you apart."Jeremiah 1:5

How these texts are used — and contested

  • Psalm 139 and Jeremiah 1:5 are widely cited by anti-abortion advocates as establishing personhood from conception. Critics note these are poetic/prophetic texts about God's intimate knowledge and special calling (Jeremiah specifically as a prophet) — not legal or definitional statements about the precise moment personhood begins, a distinction some scholars argue matters for how much theological weight the texts can bear.
  • Genesis 2:7 — "the LORD God formed a man from the dust of the ground and breathed into his nostrils the breath of life, and the man became a living being [Hebrew nephesh chayyah]" — is cited by some to argue personhood begins with breath, not conception, since the man is only called a "living being" after breathing begins.
  • Numbers 5:11–31, the "ordeal of bitter water" for a wife suspected of adultery, includes language some scholars read as describing an induced miscarriage as part of the ritual's curse if she is guilty — though this reading and its translation are themselves disputed and not universally accepted by scholars.
Scholarly Perspectives

What modern scholars and theologians say

The text of Exodus 21:22–25 is genuinely ambiguous in the Hebrew, and responsible scholarship should say so plainly rather than overstating confidence in either direction. Much of the popular debate proceeds as if the Hebrew were clear when it isn't.

John WaltonWheaton College; Old Testament scholar

The Bible's silence on induced abortion as a distinct legal category is itself significant — ancient Near Eastern law codes, including the Code of Hammurabi, did directly legislate accidental fetal harm, suggesting biblical law's relative silence reflects something about ancient Israelite assumptions we should be cautious about over-interpreting in either direction.

Bruce WaltkeReformed Theological Seminary; Hebrew Old Testament scholar

The strongest biblical argument against abortion is not any single proof text but the cumulative weight of Scripture's consistent valuing of vulnerable, voiceless life and its consistent witness against shedding innocent blood (Proverbs 6:17; Exodus 23:7).

Scott RaeTalbot School of Theology; Christian ethicist

Many evangelical and Catholic ethicists who oppose abortion also argue Scripture demands an equally robust commitment to supporting mothers, children, and families after birth — and that a consistent pro-life ethic must include economic and social justice, not focus solely on the legal status of pregnancy.

Ronald SiderEvangelicals for Social Action; author of Completely Pro-Life (1987)

Christian Positions

The major Christian positions

Full Personhood from Conception

Life and full moral status begin at fertilization

Drawing on Psalm 139, Jeremiah 1:5, and the broader biblical valuing of vulnerable life, this view holds abortion is the taking of an innocent human life at any stage, with rare exceptions debated (e.g., direct threat to the mother's life). Catholic Church, Eastern Orthodoxy, most evangelical denominations.

Gradualist / Developmental View

Moral status increases with fetal development

Some theologians argue Scripture's relative silence and the Exodus 21 ambiguity suggest full personhood may not be definitively assigned at the single moment of conception, supporting a view where moral weight increases through pregnancy, with earlier abortion carrying different (though still serious) moral weight than later abortion. Held by a minority of Christian ethicists, more common among some mainline Protestants.

Conscience & Circumstance

Abortion may be a tragic but sometimes permissible choice

Some progressive Christian traditions hold that, while abortion is never to be taken lightly, decisions in cases of rape, severe fetal anomaly, or threat to maternal life or health belong to the conscience of the woman in consultation with God, her doctor, and loved ones — not to be legislated by the state. Some UCC, Episcopal, and other mainline statements.

Consistent Life Ethic

Opposition to abortion paired with broader social commitments

Associated with Catholic social teaching (the "seamless garment" ethic) and some evangelicals, this position opposes abortion while insisting the same reverence for life requires opposing the death penalty, war, and poverty — refusing to isolate the abortion question from a wider pro-life ethic.
