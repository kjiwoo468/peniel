The Problem of Evil & Suffering

← Heaven, Hell & Salvation

Section 1

IntroductionHigh tension

If God is good and all-powerful, why does suffering exist?

The problem of evil is the oldest and most powerful intellectual challenge to theism. It is also, for many people, the most personally devastating: not an abstract puzzle but a wound. "Where was God?" is not a philosopher's question — it is the cry from Auschwitz, from the cancer ward, from the cemetery. Both the philosophical argument and the pastoral response matter here.

The Argument

Epicurus's problem and its modern form

"Is God willing to prevent evil, but not able? Then he is not omnipotent. Is he able, but not willing? Then he is malevolent. Is he both able and willing? Then whence cometh evil? Is he neither able nor willing? Then why call him God?"Attributed to Epicurus (341–270 BC), quoted in Lactantius

Two versions of the problem

  • The Logical Problem: It is logically impossible for a God who is omnipotent, omniscient, and omnibenevolent to coexist with any evil whatsoever. If God is all three of those things, evil cannot exist — but it does, therefore such a God does not exist. Most contemporary philosophers of religion consider this "deductive" version of the problem largely resolved by Alvin Plantinga's Free Will Defense.
  • The Evidential Problem: Even if the existence of some evil is compatible with God's existence in principle, the sheer amount and distribution of evil we observe — particularly intense suffering of children, natural disasters with no apparent purpose, the suffering of animals — makes it improbable that an all-powerful, all-good God exists. This version, associated with philosophers William Rowe and Paul Draper, is considered by many philosophers the stronger challenge.
The Responses

Major Christian responses to the problem

🌱

Free Will Defense (Alvin Plantinga)

God could not create beings capable of genuine love and moral goodness without also creating beings capable of choosing evil. A world with free creatures who sometimes choose evil may be more valuable than a world of "free" beings who can only choose good (which is not really freedom). This addresses moral evil (human choices) but is less compelling as a response to natural evil (earthquakes, childhood cancer).

🌿

Soul-Making Theodicy (Irenaeus / John Hick)

The world is not meant to be a paradise of pleasure but a "vale of soul-making" — a place where character is forged through challenge, suffering, and overcoming. A world without difficulty would produce no virtues of courage, compassion, or endurance. God permits suffering because maturity requires it. Critics: this seems to make children's deaths and mass atrocities into character-building exercises — a disturbing implication.

🔭

Greater Good Defense

God permits specific evils because they are necessary conditions for greater goods we may not be able to see from our limited perspective. Critics: this makes God's goodness unknowable in practice — if any atrocity might serve a hidden greater good, the concept of divine goodness becomes unfalsifiable and meaningless.

📖

The Book of Job Approach

The Bible's most sustained engagement with suffering (Job) does not explain it — God's final speeches do not answer Job's "why?" but overwhelm him with the mystery of creation's vastness. God is not absent, but his ways are beyond Job's comprehension. Genuine trust may require accepting that understanding is not on offer. This is not intellectual capitulation but a different framing of what faith does.

Scholarly Perspectives

What philosophers and theologians say

The existence of evil, when considered carefully, is actually more troubling for naturalism than for theism. If there is no God, there is no objective evil — only suffering, which is real but morally neutral. The very fact that we call the Holocaust an evil, and mean something binding and not merely a matter of taste, presupposes a moral standard that naturalism cannot ground.

Alvin PlantingaUniversity of Notre Dame; author of God, Freedom, and Evil (1974)

The Holocaust is not theodicy material. The very attempt to explain Auschwitz as serving a higher purpose — as a "soul-making" opportunity or a freedom-enabling condition — is itself a moral obscenity. The honest theological response is not explanation but lamentation: "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?"

Elie WieselHolocaust survivor; Nobel laureate; author of Night (1958)

The cross is Christianity's answer to the problem of evil — not an explanation, but a response. God does not stand outside suffering and justify it; God enters it, suffers it, and transforms it from within. The resurrection promises that suffering does not have the final word. Whether this is adequate depends on what you need the answer to do.

Jürgen MoltmannUniversity of Tübingen; author of The Crucified God (1972)

For most people, the problem of evil is not primarily an intellectual puzzle but an existential wound. The most important pastoral response is not a theodicy but presence, lament, and solidarity — sitting with those who suffer rather than explaining at them. Theodicy is for the classroom; Job's friends needed to shut up.

Nicholas WolterstorffYale Divinity School; author of Lament for a Son (1987)

Christian Responses

How Christians have engaged suffering theologically

Classical Theodicy

Suffering serves purposes within God's sovereign plan

God is sovereign over all events, including suffering; nothing happens outside his purposes; suffering serves ends — sanctification, character, bringing people to faith, or eschatological goods — that justify it from God's perspective. Often paired with Romans 8:28. Criticized for potentially trivializing extreme suffering. Reformed theology; many evangelical traditions.

Open Theism

God does not fully control all events; he grieves evil with us

God's omnipotence does not mean exhaustive control — God chose to create a world with genuine freedom and contingency, including the possibility of events that grieve God. God is not the author of evil; he works within a world where genuine evil exists and fights it with us. Preserves divine goodness at the cost of modifying omnipotence. Gregory Boyd, Clark Pinnock; significant minority in evangelical theology.

Cruciform Theodicy

The cross is God's answer — solidarity, not explanation

God's response to evil is not an explanation but an act: the incarnation and cross demonstrate that God is not aloof from suffering but enters it fully. The resurrection promises transformation, not elimination, of suffering. Faith does not require understanding God's reasons — it requires trusting the God revealed on the cross. Jürgen Moltmann; much of liberation theology; pastoral theology broadly.

Honest Lament

Some suffering remains unanswered; lament is the faithful response

The Psalms of lament (22, 44, 88), Job, and Lamentations model a response to suffering that is honest complaint rather than forced resolution. Faith does not require explanation — it permits anguished questioning while refusing to abandon God. This is itself a posture of trust. Walter Brueggemann; Nicholas Wolterstorff.
