The Bible and slavery

The Bible does not simply mention slavery in passing — it provides detailed regulations for its practice. American slaveholders cited these passages to defend chattel slavery. Abolitionists cited other passages to oppose it. Both were reading the same Bible.

Original LanguageHebrew study

Eved — a word with a wide range

עֶבֶדevedHebrew

Slave / servant / bondsman — covers a wide range of unfree and dependent labor statuses

Eved is used across the Old Testament for everything from temporary indentured servitude (paying off debt, with a maximum six-year term for Hebrews — Exodus 21:2) to permanent chattel slavery of foreigners (Leviticus 25:44–46) to a term of self-description before God ("your servant," used by kings and prophets). This breadth means a single English word ("slave" or "servant") inevitably loses nuance the original Hebrew preserved through context.

Translation comparison — Leviticus 25:44

VersionRendering
KJV"thy bondmen and thy bondmaids"
NIV (2011)"male and female slaves"
ESV"male and female slaves"
NRSV"male and female slaves"

Modern translations have moved decisively toward "slaves" rather than the softer "servants" or "bondmen" of older translations — a shift many scholars view as a more honest rendering, since this particular regulation (permanent, inheritable, foreign chattel slavery) is unambiguous in the Hebrew and should not be softened.

The Texts

What the Bible actually regulates

"If you buy a Hebrew servant, he is to serve you for six years. But in the seventh year, he shall go free, without paying anything."Exodus 21:2
"Your male and female slaves are to come from the nations around you... You can bequeath them to your children as inherited property and can make them slaves for life."Leviticus 25:44–46
"Slaves, obey your earthly masters with respect and fear, and with sincerity of heart, just as you would obey Christ."Ephesians 6:5

Honest difficulties

  • Leviticus 25 permits permanent enslavement of foreigners with no Jubilee release — not a regulation of an existing evil, but a divine permission with no built-in expiration.
  • Exodus 21:20–21: if an owner beats a slave who dies immediately, the owner is "punished" (the Hebrew penalty is unspecified, likely a fine); but if the slave survives a day or two, there's no punishment, "since the slave is the owner's property."
  • In Philemon, Paul sends the escaped Onesimus back to his owner with a letter requesting kind treatment — not commanding emancipation or condemning slavery as an institution.
  • American slaveholders were not misreading these texts. They were citing them with reasonable accuracy.
Counter-Evidence

What abolitionist Christians pointed to

"Anyone who kidnaps someone is to be put to death, whether the victim has been sold or is still in the kidnapper's possession."Exodus 21:16
"If a slave has taken refuge with you, do not hand them over to their master."Deuteronomy 23:15
"There is neither Jew nor Gentile, neither slave nor free, nor is there male and female, for you are all one in Christ Jesus."Galatians 3:28

Exodus 21:16 prescribes the death penalty for exactly the act — kidnapping a person and selling them — that founded the entire transatlantic slave trade. Deuteronomy 23:15 commands the opposite of the American Fugitive Slave Act. These texts gave Christian abolitionists, from the Quakers to Frederick Douglass (himself a biblically literate former slave and preacher), powerful scriptural ground.

Scholarly Perspectives

What modern scholars and theologians say

Scripture does not give us a timeless ethical code on slavery, but records a redemptive movement — a trajectory toward greater freedom and equality that, followed to its logical conclusion beyond the literal words of the text, leads to abolition.

William WebbAuthor of Slaves, Women & Homosexuals: Exploring the Hermeneutics of Cultural Analysis (2001)

For most of church history, including under towering theologians like Augustine, Aquinas, and Calvin, the church simply accepted slavery as a legitimate social institution. This was a profound and sustained moral failure, and honest church history must say so plainly rather than minimizing it.

Mark NollUniversity of Notre Dame; author of The Civil War as a Theological Crisis (2006)

It is important to note that Israelite "slavery" as legislated in Exodus 21 for fellow Hebrews bore little resemblance to American chattel slavery — it was closer to indentured servitude, time-limited, with significant legal protections. The Leviticus 25 permission regarding foreign slaves is the genuinely difficult outlier text that does not fit this more humane pattern.

Christopher WrightLangham Partnership; Old Testament ethicist; author of Old Testament Ethics for the People of God (2004)

The history of the church's slow journey toward recognizing slavery's evil is itself instructive: it shows that Scripture's deepest moral logic can take centuries for even devout readers to fully recognize and apply — which should make us humble about our own moral blind spots today.

Esau McCaulleyWheaton College; author of Reading While Black (2020)

Christian Positions

How Christians have understood this

Redemptive Movement Hermeneutic

The Bible moves toward abolition even if it doesn't fully arrive there

Old Testament regulations were already more humane than surrounding cultures. NT texts like Galatians 3:28 point further toward equality. The trajectory of Scripture leads to abolition. The position of most Christian abolitionists.

Cultural Accommodation

God regulated an evil he had not yet abolished

God met Israel within their existing social structures while introducing protections radically progressive for the ancient Near East — regulation, not endorsement, similar to how Jesus describes Moses "permitting" divorce "because of the hardness of your hearts" (Matthew 19:8).

Principled Critique

The seeds of abolition are in Scripture itself

Exodus 21:16's kidnapping prohibition, Deuteronomy 23:15–16's anti-fugitive-slave-law, and Galatians 3:28 point toward a fellowship in which slavery has no place. Abolitionists were reading Scripture more faithfully than slaveholders — and won the argument.

Hard Acknowledgment

The church got this wrong for a very long time

Centuries of major Christian theologians accepted slavery. Acknowledging this honestly — rather than explaining it away — is itself a form of faithfulness, and a warning against assuming the church has all moral questions right today.

See: Webb, Slaves, Women & Homosexuals (2001); Haynes, Noah's Curse (2002); Noll, The Civil War as a Theological Crisis (2006).
