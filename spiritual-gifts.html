IntroductionHigh tension

Did the miraculous gifts of the Spirit cease — or continue?

Speaking in tongues, prophecy, healing, miracles — the New Testament lists these as gifts of the Holy Spirit, displayed prominently in the early church. Today, Pentecostal and charismatic Christians — numbering over 600 million worldwide — claim these gifts continue. Many Reformed and cessationist evangelicals argue they ceased with the apostolic age. The stakes are high: one side risks inventing experiences; the other risks quenching the Spirit.

"Speaking in tongues" — but which kind?

The New Testament describes what appear to be two different phenomena both called "speaking in tongues" (glōssa): (1) Acts 2: the disciples speak and are heard in multiple known foreign languages by an international crowd — xenoglossy, actual known languages. (2) 1 Corinthians 12–14: tongues that require interpretation, described as "the tongues of angels" (1 Cor 13:1), associated with private prayer and requiring a separate gift to interpret — suggesting an ecstatic speech not necessarily corresponding to a known language. Whether these are the same gift or two distinct phenomena significantly shapes the theological debate.

"Love never fails. But where there are prophecies, they will cease; where there are tongues, they will be stilled; where there is knowledge, it will pass away."1 Corinthians 13:8

The cessationist argument from 1 Corinthians 13:10

Cessationists sometimes argue the "perfect" (to teleion) that will bring the end of gifts in 1 Corinthians 13:10 refers to the completed canon of Scripture. Continuationists respond that the broader context (vv. 11–12, "face to face," "as I am fully known") clearly refers to eschatological consummation — the same thing 1 John 3:2 and other passages point to. The "completed canon" interpretation has almost no support in patristic or medieval exegesis, and most contemporary scholars reject it across theological positions.

HistoryHistorical evidence

Cessationism as a doctrine: when did it emerge?

📜
2nd–4th centuries AD

Miraculous gifts in the patristic church

Irenaeus of Lyon (c. 180 AD), Tertullian (c. 200 AD), and Origen (c. 230 AD) all attest to continuing miraculous gifts — tongues, prophecy, healing — in their own communities. Cessationism as a systematic theological position does not appear clearly until Augustine (though even Augustine's position was nuanced), and was not clearly articulated as a doctrine until centuries later. The patristic evidence complicates the cessationist narrative that gifts were only for the apostolic period.

📚
1906

The Azusa Street Revival

The modern Pentecostal movement emerged from the Azusa Street Revival in Los Angeles, led by William Seymour — a Black preacher deeply influenced by Holiness theology. Speaking in tongues became the "initial evidence" of Spirit baptism. From this beginning, Pentecostalism grew into one of the largest Christian movements in history, now representing approximately one quarter of global Christianity and concentrated particularly in the Global South.

✨
1960s–present

The Charismatic Renewal in mainline churches

Beginning in the 1960s, charismatic renewal spread into Episcopal, Catholic, Lutheran, Presbyterian, and other mainline denominations — separating Spirit-gifts from the specifically Pentecostal framework and introducing them into traditions with more formal liturgical practices. Many Catholics and mainstream Protestants now identify as "charismatic" without identifying with Pentecostalism.

Scholarly Perspectives

What theologians say

The cessationist position rests on a single exegetical argument (1 Corinthians 13:10) that most scholars — including cessationists who are honest about the evidence — acknowledge is very difficult to sustain from the text itself. Cessationism tends to be driven by historical experience (we don't see these gifts clearly today) more than exegetical necessity.

Wayne GrudemPhoenix Seminary; author of Systematic Theology (1994) and The Gift of Prophecy in the New Testament and Today (1988); continuationist

The charismatic movement represents one of the genuine works of the Holy Spirit in the 20th century. I cannot endorse every expression of it — there is much excess and theological confusion — but the renewal of experiential encounter with the Spirit has been a genuine gift to the whole church and cannot simply be dismissed as enthusiasm or deception.

J.I. PackerRegent College; Reformed theologian; author of Keep in Step with the Spirit (1984)

Cessationism made pastoral sense in a Western church where extraordinary claims were uncommon and the main danger was credulity. It makes far less sense globally, where the church is growing fastest in exactly the contexts — poverty, illness, spiritual warfare — where the signs and wonders of Acts make most immediate sense. The theology should follow the missiology.

Lamin SannehYale Divinity School; Gambian scholar of global Christianity; author of Whose Religion Is Christianity? (2003)

Christian Positions

The major positions on spiritual gifts

Cessationism

Sign gifts ceased with the completion of the apostolic era

Tongues, prophecy, and healing miracles were given to authenticate the apostles and establish the early church. With the completion of the New Testament canon and the death of the apostles, their purpose was fulfilled and they ceased. What is claimed as tongues or prophecy today is either natural (learned behavior) or demonic. Classic Reformed and many conservative evangelical positions; John MacArthur, B.B. Warfield.

Open but Cautious (Continuationism)

Gifts continue but must be tested carefully

No clear biblical warrant for cessationism; gifts may continue, but the presence of widespread abuse, manipulation, and theological confusion in charismatic contexts requires careful testing (1 Thessalonians 5:19–21: "do not quench the Spirit... test everything"). This is a "third way" between hard cessationism and uncritical charismatic enthusiasm. Wayne Grudem, D.A. Carson, John Piper.

Classical Pentecostalism

Tongues are the initial physical evidence of Spirit baptism

A distinct "baptism in the Spirit" subsequent to conversion is available to all believers; speaking in tongues is the normative initial physical evidence of receiving it. All the gifts of 1 Corinthians 12–14 continue and are to be earnestly desired. Assemblies of God, Church of God in Christ, and most classical Pentecostal denominations.

Charismatic Renewal

All gifts continue; Spirit baptism may be a distinct experience or not

All spiritual gifts listed in the New Testament continue for the church today. Spirit baptism may be a distinct post-conversion experience (as in classical Pentecostalism) or simply the fullness of conversion's initial work — the key is openness to the Spirit's ongoing work rather than a fixed theological grid. Many Catholic charismatics, Third Wave, and broadly charismatic evangelicals.
