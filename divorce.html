Does God permit divorce — and remarriage?

Jesus speaks directly and demandingly about divorce, but the four Gospel accounts differ from each other, and Paul adds further nuance. This is a genuine interpretive puzzle with enormous pastoral stakes.

Translation Comparison

Why the Gospel accounts differ

TextException clause?Wording
Matthew 19:9Yes"...divorces his wife, except for sexual immorality, and marries another woman commits adultery"
Matthew 5:32YesSame exception clause, in the Sermon on the Mount
Mark 10:11–12No"Anyone who divorces his wife and marries another woman commits adultery against her" — no exception stated
Luke 16:18No"Anyone who divorces his wife and marries another woman commits adultery" — no exception stated

The disputed Greek word

πορνείαporneiaGreek

Sexual immorality — a broad term

Porneia is the root of the English word "pornography" but in Koine Greek had a much broader range than just "adultery" (the more specific Greek word for which is moicheia). It can denote premarital sex, incest, prostitution, or sexual immorality generally. Matthew's choice of this broader term (rather than the narrower moicheia) has fueled centuries of debate over how wide Jesus's exception actually is — limited strictly to adultery, or covering a wider range of sexual sin within marriage.

Mark and Luke's omission of any exception clause has several explanations: Mark was likely written for a Roman/Gentile audience under Roman law (where wives, unlike under Jewish law, could also initiate divorce — Mark 10:12 reflects this by addressing both directions); Matthew, writing for a Jewish-Christian audience familiar with the Pharisaic debate over Deuteronomy 24:1, may have included the clause to clarify Jesus's position within that specific debate (the schools of Hillel, who permitted divorce for almost any reason, versus Shammai, who restricted it to serious sexual sin).

The Pauline Privilege

1 Corinthians 7 — a second ground for divorce

"But if the unbeliever leaves, let it be so. The brother or the sister is not bound in such circumstances; God has called us to live in peace."1 Corinthians 7:15

Paul, addressing a situation Jesus never directly spoke to (a believer married to an unbeliever who wants to leave the marriage because of the spouse's faith), adds what later theologians dubbed the "Pauline Privilege": desertion by an unbelieving spouse is a second legitimate ground for divorce, distinct from Matthew's sexual-immorality exception. The phrase "not bound" (Greek ou dedoulōtai, literally "not enslaved") is widely understood to imply freedom to remarry, though this too is debated.

A textual note on Malachi 2:16

The famous verse "I hate divorce" is itself a translation challenge. The Hebrew is notoriously difficult and ambiguous; some scholars (including the translators of the NIV 2011 revision) now render it closer to "the man who hates and divorces his wife... does violence to the one he should protect" — shifting the condemnation from divorce itself to a husband's treacherous, abusive divorcing of a wife, which changes the verse's argument substantially.

Scholarly Perspectives

What modern scholars and theologians say

Jesus's teaching on divorce must be read against the specific first-century debate between the schools of Hillel and Shammai over the grounds for divorce in Deuteronomy 24:1. Jesus was not issuing an abstract universal law in a vacuum — he was intervening in a live rabbinic controversy.

Craig KeenerAsbury Theological Seminary; author of ...And Marries Another (1991)

Given the real and devastating presence of abuse within marriages, pastoral theology cannot responsibly treat the biblical grounds for divorce as an exhaustive, closed list. The spirit of Jesus's teaching protects the vulnerable; it does not trap victims.

David Instone-BrewerTyndale House, Cambridge; author of Divorce and Remarriage in the Bible (2002)

The new NIV rendering of Malachi 2:16 reflects a genuine and defensible reading of a difficult Hebrew text — but it should not be mistaken for the only possible reading. Both renderings remain within the bounds of responsible translation.

Douglas StuartGordon-Conwell Theological Seminary; Old Testament scholar

Christian Positions

The major positions

No Divorce / No Remarriage

Marriage is indissoluble; remarriage is adultery

Mark's account (no exception clause) gives the baseline rule; what Jesus permits in Matthew is separation, not remarriage. Catholic and Orthodox traditions use annulment instead of divorce.

Two Grounds for Divorce

Adultery and desertion permit divorce and remarriage

The Matthean exception (sexual immorality) and Pauline privilege (desertion by an unbeliever) are the only biblical grounds. Mainstream Reformed and many evangelical position.

Wider Grounds

Abuse and covenant-breaking also warrant divorce

Some theologians extend the grounds to include abuse and chronic betrayal, arguing porneia's breadth and the spirit of covenant protection support this. God does not require victims to remain in dangerous marriages.

Catholic Annulment

True marriage cannot be dissolved; some marriages were never valid

The Church grants declarations of nullity — findings that a valid sacramental marriage never existed — rather than divorces, allowing remarriage within the Church.
