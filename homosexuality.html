Homosexuality & Same-Sex Relationships

← Ethics & Human Sexuality

Section 1

IntroductionHigh tension

What does the Bible say about same-sex relationships?

Among the most contested questions in contemporary Christianity. Faithful, Scripture-serious Christians — reading the same Bible with genuine care — have reached opposing conclusions. This topic walks through all six relevant passages, the disputed Greek vocabulary, and the major positions in depth.

The Texts

The six passages that address the topic

Genesis 19Leviticus 18:22Leviticus 20:13Romans 1:26–271 Corinthians 6:91 Timothy 1:10
"Do not have sexual relations with a man as one does with a woman; that is detestable."Leviticus 18:22
"Their women exchanged natural sexual relations for unnatural ones. In the same way the men also abandoned natural relations with women and were inflamed with lust for one another."Romans 1:26–27

Genesis 19 — Sodom

Traditionally read as condemning homosexuality, the men of Sodom's demand to "know" Lot's visitors (Gen 19:5) is interpreted by most modern scholars — across the theological spectrum — as a demand for violent gang rape, an act of inhospitality and domination, not a depiction of consensual same-sex relationships. Ezekiel 16:49 identifies Sodom's primary sin as pride, excess, and failure to help the poor — not naming sexual sin at all. Most contemporary scholars on all sides now agree Genesis 19 is not directly relevant to the question of consensual same-sex relationships, though it remains a touchstone in popular discourse.

Original LanguageGreek Study

Arsenokoitai — the most disputed word

ἀρσενοκοῖταιarsenokoitaiGreek

Compound word: arsen (male) + koitē (bed/lying)

This word appears in 1 Corinthians 6:9 and 1 Timothy 1:10. It does not appear anywhere in Greek literature before Paul — he appears to have coined it, most likely by combining the two Greek words used in the Septuagint's translation of Leviticus 18:22 and 20:13 ("a male" + "lying [with]"). Because it is Paul's own compound construction rather than a word with an established usage history, its precise referent — exploitative pederasty specifically, or same-sex activity in general — is genuinely debated by Greek lexicographers.

μαλακοίmalakoiGreek

Literally "soft ones"; in context, likely the passive partner in male same-sex activity

Appearing alongside arsenokoitai in 1 Corinthians 6:9, malakoi literally means "soft." In broader Greek usage it could describe moral weakness, laziness, or luxury generally — but in vice lists alongside arsenokoitai, most scholars (including LGBTQ-affirming ones) believe it specifically denotes the receptive partner in male same-sex acts, paired with arsenokoitai as the active partner.

Translation comparison — 1 Corinthians 6:9

A notable historical case

The 1946 RSV translation of arsenokoitai as simply "homosexuals" was so controversial — collapsing a disputed ancient compound word into a modern psychological/identity category that did not exist in the ancient world — that the RSV committee revised it in the 1971 second edition. This is frequently cited by historians of sexuality (both LGBTQ-affirming and traditional) as a clear example of how translation can import modern categories backward onto ancient texts.

Historical ContextGreco-Roman background

What same-sex activity looked like in Paul's world

Understanding the first-century context is central to this debate, because both sides appeal to it for opposite conclusions.

🏛️

Pederasty was the dominant cultural pattern

The most common and culturally visible form of male same-sex activity in the Greco-Roman world was pederasty — relationships between adult men and adolescent boys, often involving significant power imbalance, and frequently economically transactional or involving enslaved boys. This is well documented in surviving Greek and Roman literature (Plato's Symposium, satirist texts, legal sources).

📜

Evidence of other forms also existed

Some ancient sources (including the Roman poet Martial and astrologer Ptolemy) describe what some classicists characterize as longer-term, more mutual same-sex relationships between adult men, though these are less extensively documented and their social status was more marginal and contested than pederasty.

The affirming position argues Paul's culturally available examples of "unnatural" same-sex activity were primarily exploitative or pederastic, and that he could not have been addressing a modern concept of committed, consensual same-sex partnership that did not exist in his social world. The traditional position responds that Paul's argument in Romans 1 is rooted in creation order ("contrary to nature," echoing Genesis 1–2's male-female pattern), not merely a critique of exploitation — making it a principled objection to the act itself, regardless of the relational context available to Paul.

Scholarly Perspectives

What modern scholars and theologians say

The Bible's negative statements about same-sex relations are unambiguous in their cumulative weight. To read them as addressing only exploitative forms requires importing a distinction the biblical authors do not themselves make.

Robert GagnonAuthor of The Bible and Homosexual Practice (2001); Pittsburgh Theological Seminary

Paul's argument in Romans 1 specifically targets people acting against their own given nature — heterosexual people engaging in homosexual acts out of excess desire, not people whose innate orientation is same-sex. This is a category Paul could not have had in view.

Matthew VinesAuthor of God and the Gay Christian (2014); founder of The Reformation Project

I hold the traditional sexual ethic, but I think both "affirming" and some "traditional" arguments often oversimplify a genuinely difficult set of ancient texts and categories. The church needs theological seriousness, not slogans, on this question.

Wesley HillTrinity School for Ministry; author of Washed and Waiting (2010); celibate gay Christian, Side B

The historical-critical evidence does not settle this question by itself. It narrows the range of responsible interpretations, but faithful, careful readers can and do still disagree about what those texts require of the church today.

James BrownsonWestern Theological Seminary; author of Bible, Gender, Sexuality (2013)

Christian Positions

The major positions today

Traditional / Historic View

All same-sex sexual activity is contrary to God's design

The six passages, read alongside the creation account's male-female pattern, consistently prohibit same-sex sexual activity. The unanimous Christian position for 2,000 years. Same-sex attraction itself is not sinful; acting on it is. Roman Catholicism, Eastern Orthodoxy, Southern Baptist Convention, most evangelical churches.

Affirming / Progressive View

The texts don't address loving, committed same-sex relationships

The ancient texts address exploitative or culturally specific same-sex acts, not modern covenantal partnerships. The biblical ethic of love and mutual self-giving applies equally to same-sex couples. Episcopal Church, ELCA, UCC, PC(USA), United Methodist Church (as of 2024).

Side B / Spiritual Friendship

Gay identity is real; gay sex is not God's design; celibacy is a high calling

Holds the traditional sexual ethic while fully affirming the experiences of gay Christians, elevating celibacy and deep non-sexual friendship as genuine vocations. Revoice movement; Wesley Hill, Eve Tushnet.

Pastoral Discernment

Welcoming, still discerning

Many congregations commit to fully welcoming LGBTQ+ people while continuing to wrestle with what Scripture teaches, resisting both rejection and quick resolution.

Resources representing multiple views: Gagnon, The Bible and Homosexual Practice (traditional); Vines, God and the Gay Christian (affirming); Hill, Washed and Waiting (Side B); Brownson, Bible, Gender, Sexuality (affirming, scholarly).
