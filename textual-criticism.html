Textual Criticism & Famous Variants

← Scripture & Interpretation

Section 1

Introduction

What textual criticism actually is

Textual criticism is the scholarly discipline of comparing ancient manuscripts to reconstruct the most likely original wording of a text. Far from undermining confidence in the Bible, it is the very tool that lets us know, with remarkable precision, where uncertainty exists — and where it doesn't.

The scale of the evidence

  • We have over 5,800 Greek New Testament manuscripts — vastly more than for any other ancient text (compare: about 650 manuscripts of Homer's Iliad, the next best-attested ancient work).
  • The earliest virtually complete New Testament manuscripts (Codex Sinaiticus and Codex Vaticanus) date to the 4th century — within roughly 300 years of the autographs, a remarkably short gap by ancient standards.
  • Of the 200,000+ variants across all manuscripts, the overwhelming majority are spelling differences, word-order changes, or obvious slips that affect no translation and no doctrine.
Famous Variant 1Disputed passage

John 7:53–8:11 — the Pericope Adulterae

"Let any one of you who is without sin be the first to throw a stone at her."John 8:7

Manuscript evidence

This beloved passage is absent from the oldest and most reliable Greek manuscripts — including Papyrus 66 and Papyrus 75 (both c. 200 AD), and Codex Sinaiticus and Codex Vaticanus (4th century). When it does appear in later manuscripts, it appears in different locations — sometimes after John 7:36, sometimes after John 21:25, and in one manuscript family, after Luke 21:38. Its vocabulary and style differ noticeably from the rest of John's Gospel.

Nearly all textual scholars — including conservative evangelical ones — conclude this passage was not originally part of John's Gospel, but was added later, likely preserving a genuine early tradition about Jesus that circulated independently before being inserted into John at some point in the 4th–5th century. Most modern translations (NIV, ESV, NRSV) retain the passage but bracket it with an explanatory footnote.

Famous Variant 2Disputed passage

Mark 16:9–20 — the "longer ending"

Manuscript evidence

The two earliest complete manuscripts of Mark (Codex Sinaiticus and Codex Vaticanus, both 4th century) end abruptly at Mark 16:8: "they were afraid." No resurrection appearance, no Great Commission. Other manuscripts add a "shorter ending" (a brief paragraph) or the familiar "longer ending" (verses 9–20, including snake-handling and poison-drinking — Mark 16:18 — that some Appalachian Pentecostal churches still practice based on this passage).

The vocabulary of the longer ending differs notably from the rest of Mark, and church father Eusebius (4th century) noted that "the accurate copies" of Mark ended at verse 8. Most scholars conclude the longer ending was added later — possibly compiled from material in the other Gospels and Acts — though debate continues over whether Mark's original ending was lost (perhaps the last page of a scroll) or whether verse 8 was the intended, dramatically abrupt conclusion.

Why this matters practically

Snake-handling churches (associated with Appalachian Pentecostalism, including some Kentucky congregations) base the practice directly on Mark 16:18 — a passage the majority of textual scholars believe was not originally part of Mark's Gospel. This is a vivid example of how textual-critical questions have direct, real-world religious consequences.

Famous Variant 3Trinitarian proof-text

1 John 5:7–8 — the clearest Trinity verse that may not be original

VersionText
KJV (1611)"For there are three that bear record in heaven, the Father, the Word, and the Holy Ghost: and these three are one. And there are three that bear witness in earth..."
NIV / ESV / NRSV"For there are three that testify: the Spirit, the water and the blood; and the three are in agreement." (no mention of Father/Word/Spirit "in heaven")

The "Comma Johanneum" — a famous later addition

The explicitly Trinitarian phrase ("the Father, the Word, and the Holy Ghost... these three are one") appears in no Greek manuscript earlier than the 14th century, and is absent from all the earliest Latin manuscripts as well. It appears to have originated as a marginal theological gloss in Latin Christianity, possibly in North Africa in the 4th–5th century, that was eventually copied into the main text. Erasmus, compiling his Greek New Testament (the basis for the KJV) in 1516, initially omitted it for lack of Greek manuscript support — then included it in his 1522 edition after facing pressure, having been shown (likely fabricated) a single late Greek manuscript containing it.

This is significant: it means the most explicit single-verse statement of the Trinity in the KJV is, by the consensus of modern textual scholarship, not part of the original text of 1 John. This does not undermine the doctrine of the Trinity — which rests on the cumulative witness of many other texts — but it is a striking case study in how a single verse entered Scripture through transmission rather than original authorship.

Printing HistoryHistorical record

When the printing press introduced errors

🖨️
1631, London

The "Wicked Bible" (Sinners' Bible)

Printers Robert Barker and Martin Lucas omitted "not" from Exodus 20:14, printing: "Thou shalt commit adultery." King Charles I and Archbishop Laud were furious; the printers were fined £300 (a fortune at the time) and stripped of their printing license. Most copies were destroyed by royal order; about 11 survive today, including one in the U.S. Library of Congress.

🖨️
1716, England

The "Sin On" Bible (Vinegar Bible)

John 8:11 was printed "Go and sin on more" instead of "sin no more." A separate 1717 printing famously mistitled the parable of the vineyard as the "Parable of the Vinegar."

🖨️
1631, England

The "Unrighteous Bible"

1 Corinthians 6:9 was printed as "the unrighteous shall inherit the kingdom of God" — the exact opposite of Paul's meaning, omitting "not."

These printing errors are well-documented historical curiosities, not evidence about the reliability of the underlying manuscript tradition — they were caught, corrected, and the offending editions destroyed or recalled. But they illustrate vividly that the physical production of Bibles has always been a human process subject to human error, even centuries after the canon was settled.

Reflection

What textual criticism teaches us

Of all the 200,000-plus variants among New Testament manuscripts, only a small fraction are even viable candidates for the original reading, and of those, none affects any major Christian doctrine. We can reconstruct the text with a confidence that would be the envy of any other ancient document.

Daniel WallaceDallas Theological Seminary; founder of the Center for the Study of New Testament Manuscripts

The fact that scholars can identify, with high confidence, exactly which verses are later additions — and that mainstream Bible translations openly footnote them — should increase trust in the field, not undermine it. This is transparency, not cover-up.

Bart EhrmanUniversity of North Carolina; author of Misquoting Jesus (2005) — though Ehrman draws more skeptical theological conclusions than most evangelical textual critics
